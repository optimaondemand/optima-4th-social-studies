🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Honesty
Honesty in Florida means telling the truth about the weather — not pretending hurricanes won't come, not promising a sunny day to a worried neighbor. Maps, forecasts, and warnings only work when the people making them are honest about the risks. Today, as you trace how weather shapes Florida life, notice how every good decision starts with honest information.

Word Roots
Greek Root: hydro — "water"

The Greek root hydro means "water." In Florida, with its rain, rivers, springs, and wetlands, this root shows up everywhere — and it shows up especially when we talk about how weather shapes life.

📖

hydrate — to add water · dehydrate — to remove water · hydrant — a water outlet for firefighters · hydroelectric — electricity made from moving water

🔎 Discovery Fact
The word "hydrate" comes from hydro (water) — to add water to something. Florida farmers "hydrate" their orange trees during dry weeks so the fruit doesn't shrivel.

What Florida's Climate Does to Florida Life
Agriculture, building, preparation, and tourism

Florida's climate isn't just a backdrop — it shapes nearly every choice people make. Citrus farmers plant in central Florida because the soil drains well and frost is rare. Cattle ranchers spread out across the dry interior. Sprinkler systems and irrigation keep crops alive during the driest weeks. When summer downpours flood low ground, drainage canals carry water out of cities and farmland.

Florida homes are often built with hurricane shutters, sturdy roofs, and tie-downs that hold structures in place during high winds. Coastal towns plan evacuation routes in advance so people can leave safely if a storm is coming. The state's biggest industry — tourism — also runs on climate: warm winters bring visitors from cold states, and beach resorts depend on the weather being mostly cooperative.

Quick Self-Check
🧠

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. Why are hurricane shutters common on Florida homes?
ABecause they keep the house cool in winter
BBecause they protect windows from high winds and flying debris during storms
CBecause they let in more sunlight
Correct! Hurricane shutters cover windows during a storm so wind and flying debris can't break the glass. They're a direct response to Florida's hurricane season.
Think about what shutters cover and why a storm would be dangerous to a window. What's the weather causing this design choice?
2. Why is central Florida a major citrus-growing region?
ABecause frost is rare and the soil drains well
BBecause the coast is too crowded
CBecause hurricanes never reach the middle of the state
Correct! Citrus trees can't survive hard frost, and central Florida's mild winters and well-drained sandy soil are nearly perfect for them.
What does citrus need to grow well — and what does Florida's central region offer that other parts of the country don't?
3. What is a cause-and-effect link between climate and a Florida choice?
AHurricane season → evacuation routes are planned in advance
BThe state seal → Florida's name
CThe capital is Tallahassee → there are highways
Correct! Hurricane season is the cause; planning evacuation routes is the effect. A climate pattern is shaping a human choice.
Look for a pair where the weather is the cause and a human decision is the effect. Which choice has weather doing the causing?
