Knowledge Check
1What does a historian study?
  • A) Only famous people from long ago
  • B) People, places, and events from the past ✓
  • C) Only wars and battles
  • D) Things that will happen in the future
Correct Answer
B) A person who studies people, places, and events from the past.
Common Misconception
Students may confuse historian with "archaeologist" or think historians only study famous people or wars.
2Which of these is a primary source?
  • A) A textbook about the Seminole Wars
  • B) A Wikipedia article about Florida
  • C) A letter written by a soldier during the Seminole Wars ✓
  • D) A documentary film made in 2020
Correct Answer
C) A letter written by a soldier during the Seminole Wars. It was created by someone who was there at the time.
Common Misconception
Students pick the textbook option thinking "official" = primary. Remind them: primary sources come from the time period itself.
3Which of these is a secondary source?
  • A) A diary entry from 1845
  • B) A textbook chapter about Florida's history ✓
  • C) An old photograph from 1900
  • D) A handwritten map drawn by an explorer
Correct Answer
B) A textbook chapter about Florida's history. It was written later, by someone who studied the events but was not there.
Common Misconception
Students may think any old document is secondary. Emphasize that age alone does not determine source type — what matters is whether the creator was there.
4What is the difference between print and electronic media?
  • A) Print sources are physical (books, newspapers); electronic sources are digital (websites, databases) ✓
  • B) Print sources are always older than electronic sources
  • C) Electronic sources are always more accurate than print sources
  • D) There is no real difference between them
Correct Answer
A) Print sources are physical (books, newspapers); electronic sources are digital (websites, databases).
Common Misconception
Students may think "electronic" means TV only, or that electronic sources are always more trustworthy.
5What does "chronology" mean?
  • A) The study of ancient clocks
  • B) A type of history book
  • C) A list of important people
  • D) The order in which events happened over time ✓
Correct Answer
D) The order in which events happened over time.
Common Misconception
Students confuse chronology with "biography" or think it has to do with clocks because of the "chron-" root.
6The word root "arch-" means:
  • A) Important or powerful
  • B) Ancient or beginning ✓
  • C) To build or construct
  • D) To write or record
Correct Answer
B) Ancient or beginning. This root appears in words like archaeology and archive.
Common Misconception
Students may guess "important" or "building" — remind them of the connection to archaeology (study of ancient things).
7What is an artifact?
  • A) A type of history book
  • B) A modern tool used by scientists
  • C) An object made or used by people in the past ✓
  • D) A story passed down through families
Correct Answer
C) An object made or used by people in the past.
Common Misconception
Students may think only very old things (thousands of years) count as artifacts. An artifact can be from any past time period.
8What does "evidence" mean for historians?
  • A) Information that helps prove or support a claim ✓
  • B) A guess about what happened
  • C) A story someone tells about the past
  • D) A picture drawn by an artist
Correct Answer
A) Information that helps prove or support a claim.
Common Misconception
Students may think evidence means "opinion" or confuse it with a guess. Stress that evidence is factual information used to back up a claim.

Section B
Source & Map Task
📄
Source Scenario

A historian finds an old photograph of a Florida town from 1920. Next to it is a newspaper article from 2010 describing the same town.

9Which source is primary and which is secondary? Explain how you know.
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
The photograph from 1920 is a primary source because it was created at the time it shows — someone took that picture in 1920 while they were actually there. The newspaper article from 2010 is a secondary source because it was written 90 years later by someone who was not there in 1920. The writer of the article learned about the town by studying other sources, not by being there.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Correctly identifies photograph as primary and article as secondary; gives a clear reason for each (created at the time vs. created later). Developing (2): Identifies both correctly but only explains one, or explanation is vague. Beginning (1): Mislabels one or both sources, or provides no explanation.
10What kind of evidence could the photograph give that the newspaper article cannot?
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
The photograph could show exactly what the town looked like in 1920 — the buildings, the streets, what people were wearing, and what kinds of vehicles or animals were around. This is evidence you can see with your own eyes. The newspaper article from 2010 can only describe the town in words, and the writer might not know every detail about how things looked back then. The photograph captures a real moment that cannot be changed.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Names at least two specific types of visual evidence (buildings, clothing, streets, etc.) and explains why a photograph captures what words cannot. Developing (2): Names one type of evidence or gives a general answer like "you can see what it looked like." Beginning (1): Cannot articulate what a photograph provides or gives an unrelated answer.
11If you wanted to put these sources in chronological order, which comes first and why?
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
The photograph from 1920 comes first in chronological order because it was created before the newspaper article from 2010. Chronological order means putting things in the order they happened, from earliest to latest. Since 1920 is 90 years before 2010, the photograph goes first on a timeline.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Correctly places photograph first, states the dates, and explains what chronological order means. Developing (2): Correctly places photograph first but does not explain what chronological order means. Beginning (1): Places sources in the wrong order or does not attempt the question.
C
Section C
Socratic Response
12Imagine you found an old letter written by a Florida farmer in 1850 and a textbook chapter about Florida farming. Compare these two sources. Which would tell you more about what daily life was really like? Use at least two pieces of evidence from what you learned about primary and secondary sources.
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
The letter written by the Florida farmer in 1850 would tell me more about what daily life was really like. First, the letter is a primary source because the farmer wrote it while actually living that life — he could describe what he saw, felt, and did every day, like what crops he planted or what the weather was like. Second, primary sources give us firsthand evidence, which means the information comes straight from someone who was there. The textbook is a secondary source written much later by someone who was not a farmer in 1850. The textbook might have good general information, but it cannot capture the small, personal details that make daily life real — like how tired the farmer felt or what he ate for dinner. That is why primary sources are so valuable for understanding what life was really like.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Chooses the letter; provides at least 2 pieces of evidence about why primary sources reveal daily life better (e.g., firsthand account, personal details, written at the time); compares to the textbook. Developing (2): Chooses the letter but only provides 1 piece of evidence, or comparison to the textbook is weak. Beginning (1): Chooses the textbook without reasoning, or provides no evidence from lessons. Accept the textbook if the student provides strong reasoning (e.g., "it summarizes many sources"), but this is rare at this level.
13Why is it important for historians to put events in chronological order? Give an example of how mixing up the order of events could change the story.
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
It is important for historians to put events in chronological order because the order helps us understand cause and effect — what happened first can explain why something else happened next. If historians mix up the order, the whole story can change. For example, if someone said Florida became a state and then Spain gave Florida to the United States, that would not make sense. In real life, Spain gave Florida to the United States first (in 1821), and then Florida became a state later (in 1845). Mixing up those events would confuse people about why and how Florida became part of the United States.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Explains that chronological order shows cause and effect or helps the story make sense; provides a clear, specific example of how mixing up order changes meaning. Developing (2): Explains importance but example is vague or generic ("things would be confusing"). Beginning (1): Cannot explain why order matters or provides no example.

Section D
Virtue Lens Reflection
⭐ Unit Virtue — Honesty
Honesty means telling the truth and being fair, even when it is difficult. Historians must be honest about the evidence they find.
14The virtue for this unit is honesty. Why is honesty important for a historian? What could happen if a historian was not honest about the evidence they found?
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
Honesty is important for a historian because people trust historians to tell the true story of the past. If a historian finds evidence that does not match what they expected, they still need to share it honestly. If a historian was not honest — for example, if they hid evidence or changed what a source said — then people would learn a wrong version of history. Other historians might build on that wrong information, and the mistake would spread. Honest record-keeping makes sure that the stories we learn about the past are as true and fair as possible.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Clearly explains why honesty matters in historical work and gives a specific consequence of dishonesty (e.g., wrong history, people misled, mistakes spread). Developing (2): Mentions honesty is important but explanation lacks depth or the consequence is generic ("bad things would happen"). Beginning (1): Does not connect honesty to the work of a historian or provides no consequence.
