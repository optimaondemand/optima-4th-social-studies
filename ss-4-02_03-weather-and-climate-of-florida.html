🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Responsibility
Florida's weather can change a day fast — a sunny morning can turn into an afternoon thunderstorm, and hurricane season runs from June through November every year. Being responsible in Florida means checking the forecast, knowing the warning signs, and preparing before the storm. Today, as you study Florida's climate, notice how much of responsible Florida life is built around weather you can't control.

Word Roots
Greek Root: thermo — "heat"

The Greek root thermo means "heat." Florida geographers and scientists use words built on it whenever they talk about temperature, energy, or the warmth of a place.

📖

thermometer — a tool that measures heat · thermal — relating to heat · thermos — a container that keeps heat in · geothermal — heat from inside the earth

🔎 Discovery Fact
The word "thermometer" combines thermo (heat) and meter (measure) — literally, "heat-measurer." Florida geographers use thermometers all year to track how the state stays warm even in winter.

Florida's Climate — the Invisible Layer
Heat, rain, sun, and hurricane season

Florida sits far enough south that the sun stays strong all year. Most days are warm; most winters are mild. But Florida is not a one-weather state. Afternoon thunderstorms roll through almost every summer day, dropping heavy rain fast. The state has one of the highest rainfall totals in the country, which is why the Everglades stay wet and so many lakes and springs exist.

From June through November, Florida enters hurricane season — months when powerful storms spin in over the warm ocean and sometimes strike the coast. Florida's climate (the long-term pattern of weather) and its weather (what's happening today) are not the same thing. A climate map shows you the patterns over years; a weather map shows you what's happening right now.

Connect the Ideas
💡 Key Concept

Florida's climate is warm and rainy with a long hurricane season. A climate map shows patterns over years, not just today — and those patterns shape every choice Floridians make about where to live, what to build, and how to prepare.

🦉
🧭 Opti says…
Quick observation: notice that climate is invisible until you map it. Without a climate map, Florida just looks like a sunny peninsula. With the lens, you can see rainfall lines, temperature zones, and the curving paths hurricanes have taken over many years. Geographers trust the map because it shows the pattern — not just what one sunny afternoon looks like.

Quick Self-Check

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. What does Florida's climate look like overall?
ACold and dry most of the year
BWarm and rainy with a hurricane season
CCool and snowy in winter
Correct! Florida's climate is warm and rainy, with a clear hurricane season from June through November.
Look back at the explore section. Florida is far south, so the sun is strong all year. What does that mean for temperature and rainfall?
2. What is the difference between weather and climate?
AWeather is today's conditions; climate is the long-term pattern
BWeather is for north Florida; climate is for south Florida
CThere is no real difference
Correct! Weather is what's happening right now or today. Climate is the long-term pattern over many years.
Think about the word "climate." A geographer uses it to talk about patterns that show up over years, not just one day. What does that make weather, by contrast?
3. When is Florida's hurricane season?
AJanuary through April
BAll year long
CJune through November
Correct! Hurricane season runs from June through November — the months when the ocean is warm enough to fuel powerful storms.
Hurricanes need warm ocean water. Which months of the year would the Atlantic and Gulf be warmest? That's when hurricanes are most likely.
