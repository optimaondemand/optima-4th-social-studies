Knowledge Check
1Where did the Calusa people live?
  • A) In the mountains of northern Florida
  • B) Along Florida's southwest coast ✓
  • C) In the Florida Keys only
  • D) Near present-day Jacksonville
Correct Answer
B) Along Florida's southwest coast.
Common Misconception
Students may confuse Calusa territory with other groups. The Calusa were coastal people on the Gulf side of southern Florida.
2What is a midden?
  • A) A type of canoe used by Native peoples
  • B) A mound of shells, bones, and trash that tells us about daily life ✓
  • C) A stone tool for cutting
  • D) A meeting place for tribal leaders
Correct Answer
B) A mound of shells, bones, and trash that tells us about daily life.
Common Misconception
Students may think middens are just garbage piles. Emphasize they are valuable evidence of diet, tools, and daily habits.
3What is the difference between an observation and an inference?
  • A) They mean the same thing
  • B) An observation is what you can directly see; an inference is a conclusion based on evidence ✓
  • C) An observation is a guess; an inference is a fact
  • D) An inference comes first, then an observation
Correct Answer
B) An observation is what you can directly see; an inference is a conclusion based on evidence.
Common Misconception
Students often mix these up. Observation = 'I see fish scales on the tool.' Inference = 'This tool was probably used for cutting fish.'
4Which Native group built large mounds and was known for fishing and shell tools?
  • A) Timucua
  • B) Tequesta
  • C) Apalachee
  • D) Calusa ✓
Correct Answer
D) The Calusa. They were master fishers and builders who used shells for tools and constructed large shell mounds.
Common Misconception
Students may confuse the Calusa with the Apalachee (who were farmers) or the Timucua.
5The Apalachee lived in which part of Florida?
  • A) The Florida Keys
  • B) The southeast coast near Miami
  • C) The Panhandle region of northwest Florida ✓
  • D) The central lakes region
Correct Answer
C) The Panhandle region of northwest Florida.
Common Misconception
Students may place the Apalachee in the wrong region. Connect the name to the Panhandle and farming.
6What does the word 'artifact' mean?
  • A) A natural rock formation
  • B) A type of plant used for medicine
  • C) An object made or used by people in the past ✓
  • D) A written record of events
Correct Answer
C) An object made or used by people in the past.
Common Misconception
Students may think artifacts must be ancient or only refer to tools. Any human-made object from the past counts.
7The Timucua are known for which type of historical evidence?
  • A) Pottery found in caves
  • B) The de Bry engravings showing their villages ✓
  • C) Stone tablets with writing
  • D) Spoken stories recorded on tape
Correct Answer
B) The de Bry engravings showing their villages.
Common Misconception
Students may not remember de Bry by name. Remind them these are the detailed drawings showing Timucua village life.
8Where did the Tequesta people live?
  • A) In northwest Florida
  • B) Along the Suwannee River
  • C) In the central highlands
  • D) In southeast Florida, near present-day Miami ✓
Correct Answer
D) In southeast Florida, near present-day Miami.
Common Misconception
Students may confuse Tequesta with Calusa since both were in southern Florida. Tequesta = southeast (Atlantic coast); Calusa = southwest (Gulf coast).

1 point per correct answer. Total: 8 points. Secure = 7–8, Developing = 5–6, Beginning = 0–4.
B
Section B
Source & Artifact Task
📄
Source Scenario

A historian is studying a shell tool found at a Calusa site near the coast. The tool has a sharp edge and fish scales still stuck to it.

Use what you know about artifacts, observations, and inferences to answer the questions below.
9List two observations you can make about this artifact — things you can directly see or measure.
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
Two observations: (1) The tool is made from a shell and has a sharp edge. (2) There are fish scales stuck to the tool. These are both things you can see directly without making any guesses.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Lists 2 clear, factual observations that could be directly seen (material, shape, size, fish scales, sharp edge). Developing (2): Lists 1 observation or mixes in an inference. Beginning (1): Cannot list observations or only provides inferences.
10Now make one inference about how this tool was used. Explain what evidence supports your inference.
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
I infer that this shell tool was used for cutting or cleaning fish. My evidence is that the tool has a sharp edge (good for cutting) and there are fish scales still stuck to it (which means it touched fish). The sharp edge plus the fish scales together support my inference that this was a fishing tool.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Makes a reasonable inference and cites specific evidence from the artifact description (sharp edge + fish scales). Developing (2): Makes an inference but evidence is vague or only one piece cited. Beginning (1): Cannot make an inference or provides no supporting evidence.
11Why is it important that this artifact was found near the coast? How does location help historians understand the Calusa?
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
The location is important because the Calusa lived along the coast and depended on the ocean for food. Finding a fish-cutting tool near the coast confirms that the Calusa were fishers who used the ocean's resources. If this same tool were found far inland, historians might draw different conclusions. Location helps historians connect artifacts to the people who made them and understand how those people used their environment.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Explains that coastal location connects to Calusa lifestyle (fishing, ocean resources) and why location matters for interpretation. Developing (2): Mentions coast but does not fully explain why location matters for understanding a culture. Beginning (1): Does not connect location to the Calusa or ignores the question.
C
Section C
Socratic Response
12Compare the Calusa and the Apalachee. How did their environments shape the way they found food, built shelter, and made tools? Use specific examples from what you learned.
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
The Calusa lived on the southwest coast, so their environment was shaped by the ocean, estuaries, and mangroves. They got food mainly by fishing and collecting shellfish. They built their homes on shell mounds and used shells to make tools like hammers and cutting blades. The Apalachee lived in the Panhandle region of northwest Florida, where the land was fertile with rolling hills and forests. They were farmers who grew corn, beans, and squash. They built more permanent villages with wooden structures. Their tools were made from wood and stone because those materials were available in their forested environment. Both groups adapted to what their environment provided — the Calusa used the sea, and the Apalachee used the land.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Compares both groups with specific examples of food, shelter, and tools connected to their environments. Developing (2): Describes one group well but the other is vague, or only covers 1-2 categories instead of all three. Beginning (1): Cannot compare the groups or does not connect lifestyle to environment.
13Why do historians study artifacts and middens instead of just reading books about Native peoples? What can physical evidence tell us that written sources cannot?
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
Historians study artifacts and middens because Florida's Native peoples did not have a written language, so there are no books or documents from their perspective. Artifacts and middens are the primary evidence we have. A midden can tell us what people ate, what tools they used, and how their daily life changed over time — details that no later book could fully capture. Physical evidence is also more direct: a shell tool found at a Calusa site is proof that they used shell tools, not just someone's opinion about it. Written sources about Native peoples were usually created much later by outsiders who may not have fully understood the culture.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Explains that Native peoples had no written language; names specific things artifacts/middens reveal (diet, tools, daily life); notes the directness of physical evidence vs. later accounts. Developing (2): Mentions one reason but does not elaborate or gives only a general answer. Beginning (1): Cannot explain why physical evidence matters.

Section D
Virtue Lens Reflection
⭐ Unit Virtue — Courtesy & Responsibility
Courtesy means treating others with respect and kindness. Responsibility means doing your part carefully and thoughtfully. When we study other cultures, we must do so with both.
14The virtues for this unit are courtesy and responsibility. Why is it important to study Native peoples with courtesy and respect? What responsibility do we have when learning about cultures different from our own?
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
It is important to study Native peoples with courtesy because these were real people with rich, complex cultures. If we study them carelessly or make fun of their ways of life, we disrespect their memory and their living descendants. Courtesy means we describe their cultures accurately and respectfully, without judging them by modern standards. Our responsibility is to learn the truth — to use evidence carefully, represent their cultures honestly, and remember that studying another culture is a privilege. We should ask, 'What can I learn?' rather than 'How were they different from me?' Responsible learning means we treat every culture's story as valuable and worth understanding.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Connects both courtesy and responsibility to specific actions when studying cultures (accuracy, respect, not judging, using evidence carefully). Developing (2): Mentions respect but reasoning is vague or only addresses one virtue. Beginning (1): Does not connect virtues to cultural study.
