Section A
Knowledge Check
1Who was the first European explorer to reach Florida in 1513?
  • A) Hernando de Soto
  • B) Juan Ponce de León ✓
  • C) Pánfilo de Narváez
  • D) Pedro Menéndez de Avilés
Correct Answer
B) Juan Ponce de León. He arrived in Florida in 1513 and named it 'La Florida.'
Common Misconception
Students may confuse Ponce de León with de Soto or Narváez. Ponce de León = first to arrive (1513); de Soto = long expedition through the Southeast.
2What were the three main motives for Spanish exploration, often called 'God, Gold, and Glory'?
  • A) Farming, fishing, and freedom
  • B) Spreading religion, finding wealth, and gaining fame for Spain ✓
  • C) Building schools, hospitals, and churches
  • D) Trading with Native peoples, learning languages, and mapping
Correct Answer
B) Spreading religion (God), finding wealth (Gold), and gaining fame for Spain (Glory).
Common Misconception
Students may remember the phrase but not what each word represents. Connect each to specific actions: missions (God), treasure hunting (Gold), claiming land (Glory).
3What is a mission?
  • A) A type of Spanish warship
  • B) A fort built to protect a harbor
  • C) A religious settlement where Spanish priests tried to convert Native peoples to Christianity ✓
  • D) A trading post run by French fur traders
Correct Answer
C) A religious settlement where Spanish priests tried to convert Native peoples to Christianity.
Common Misconception
Students may confuse missions with forts. Missions were primarily religious; forts were military.
4Which European country built Fort Caroline in Florida?
  • A) Spain
  • B) England
  • C) France ✓
  • D) Portugal
Correct Answer
C) France. Fort Caroline was a French settlement that threatened Spanish control of Florida.
Common Misconception
Students may guess Spain since most Florida exploration was Spanish. Fort Caroline was the French challenge that led Spain to build St. Augustine.
5What is the oldest permanent European settlement in the United States?
  • A) Jamestown
  • B) Plymouth
  • C) St. Augustine ✓
  • D) Fort Caroline
Correct Answer
C) St. Augustine, founded by Pedro Menéndez de Avilés in 1565.
Common Misconception
Students often guess Jamestown (1607) or Plymouth (1620). St. Augustine predates both by decades.
6What does the word 'colonization' mean?
  • A) Exploring a new place for the first time
  • B) One country taking control of a land and sending settlers to live there ✓
  • C) Trading goods between two countries
  • D) Drawing maps of unknown territory
Correct Answer
B) One country taking control of a land and sending settlers to live there.
Common Misconception
Students may confuse exploration (visiting) with colonization (taking control and settling). Exploration can happen without colonization.
7Which explorer led a long, difficult expedition through Florida and the Southeast in the 1530s–1540s?
  • A) Juan Ponce de León
  • B) Pedro Menéndez de Avilés
  • C) Pánfilo de Narváez
  • D) Hernando de Soto ✓
Correct Answer
D) Hernando de Soto. His expedition (1539–1542) traveled through Florida and much of the Southeast.
Common Misconception
Students may confuse de Soto with Narváez (who also had a disastrous Florida expedition but earlier, in 1528). De Soto's was the longer, more extensive journey.
8Why did Spain, France, and England compete for control of Florida?
  • A) They all wanted to build vacation resorts
  • B) Florida had the best farmland in the world
  • C) Florida's location gave control over shipping routes and trade ✓
  • D) They were all trying to find a passage to China
Correct Answer
C) Florida's location gave control over shipping routes and trade. Whoever controlled Florida could protect or threaten ships moving through the Gulf of Mexico and along the Atlantic coast.
Common Misconception
Students may think it was only about gold or land. Emphasize the strategic importance of Florida's position for shipping and military control.
Section A Scoring
1 point per correct answer. Total: 8 points. Secure = 7–8, Developing = 5–6, Beginning = 0–4.
B
Section B
Source & Map Task
📄
Source Scenario

Read this adapted excerpt from a Spanish Royal Decree of 1573, which gave instructions to explorers:

"Those who go on expeditions shall seek to spread our Holy Faith among the native peoples. They shall also claim new lands for the Crown of Spain and search for gold, silver, and other precious resources."

Use this source and what you learned to answer the questions below.

9What three goals does this decree describe? How do they connect to 'God, Gold, and Glory'?
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
The decree describes three goals: (1) Spread the Holy Faith among Native peoples — this is 'God,' meaning the Spanish wanted to convert Native peoples to Christianity. (2) Claim new lands for the Crown of Spain — this is 'Glory,' meaning Spain wanted to become more powerful and famous by controlling more territory. (3) Search for gold, silver, and precious resources — this is 'Gold,' meaning Spain wanted wealth from the new lands. Each goal matches one part of the 'God, Gold, and Glory' motives that drove Spanish exploration.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Identifies all three goals from the text and correctly matches each to God, Gold, or Glory with explanation. Developing (2): Identifies 2 goals or matches are incomplete. Beginning (1): Cannot identify the goals or does not connect them to the motives.
10Is this decree a primary source or a secondary source? Explain how you know.
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
This decree is a primary source because it was written during the time of exploration by the Spanish government. It is an official document from 1573 that gives real instructions to explorers. It was created by people who were directly involved in the decisions about exploration. A secondary source would be a modern textbook describing what the decree said.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Correctly identifies it as primary; explains it was created during the time period by someone directly involved. Developing (2): Correct identification but explanation is vague. Beginning (1): Misidentifies the source type or gives no explanation.
11How might a Native person living in Florida in the 1500s have felt about these instructions? Explain using evidence from what you learned.
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
A Native person might have felt afraid, angry, or threatened by these instructions. The decree says explorers should spread their religion, which means they would try to change Native peoples' beliefs and traditions. It also says they should claim land — land that Native peoples already lived on and used. And searching for gold and resources could mean taking things that belonged to Native communities. From what we learned, colonization often brought disease, forced labor, and the destruction of Native cultures. A Native person would have good reason to see these instructions as a threat to their way of life.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Considers the Native perspective with empathy; uses at least 2 pieces of evidence (religion change, land claiming, resource taking, disease, forced labor). Developing (2): Attempts the perspective but uses only 1 piece of evidence or is vague. Beginning (1): Cannot consider the Native perspective or gives no evidence.
C
Section C
Socratic Response
12Explain the causes and effects of Spanish colonization in Florida. What caused Spain to colonize Florida, and what were at least two effects on the land and its people?
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
Spain colonized Florida for several causes: they wanted to spread Christianity (God), find gold and silver (Gold), and claim territory to increase Spain's power (Glory). They also needed to protect their shipping routes from France and England. The effects of colonization were significant. First, Native peoples suffered greatly — European diseases like smallpox killed many people who had no immunity. Second, missions were built where Native peoples were forced or pressured to adopt Spanish religion and customs, changing their traditional way of life. Third, Spain established permanent settlements like St. Augustine, which changed the landscape and began the long process of European control over Florida.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Names clear causes (God/Gold/Glory, shipping routes) and 2+ specific effects (disease, missions/cultural change, settlements, forced labor). Developing (2): Names causes but only 1 effect, or effects are vague. Beginning (1): Cannot explain causes or effects of colonization.
13Put these events in chronological order and explain why the order matters: (a) Spain builds St. Augustine, (b) France builds Fort Caroline, (c) Ponce de León arrives in Florida.
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
The correct chronological order is: (c) Ponce de León arrives in Florida (1513) → (b) France builds Fort Caroline (1564) → (a) Spain builds St. Augustine (1565). The order matters because each event caused the next. First, Ponce de León's arrival started Spanish interest in Florida. Then, decades later, France built Fort Caroline, which threatened Spanish control. Because of that French threat, Spain sent Pedro Menéndez de Avilés to build St. Augustine and destroy Fort Caroline. If you mix up the order, you cannot understand why Spain built St. Augustine — it was a direct response to the French threat.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Correct order with dates; explains cause-and-effect chain between events. Developing (2): Correct order but cause-effect explanation is incomplete. Beginning (1): Wrong order or no explanation of why order matters.

Section D
Virtue Lens Reflection
⭐ Unit Virtue — Courage
Courage means facing something difficult or unknown with bravery. Explorers showed courage — but courage does not always mean an action is right. We must also weigh the costs.
14The virtue for this unit is courage. Explorers showed courage by sailing into unknown waters. But colonization also caused great harm to Native peoples. Can an action be courageous and also cause harm? Explain your thinking.
Model Answer (Secure — 3)
Yes, an action can be courageous and still cause harm. The explorers were brave — they sailed across oceans into unknown territory, faced storms, hunger, and danger. That took real courage. But courage does not make an action right. The same explorers who showed courage also colonized lands that belonged to Native peoples, spread diseases, forced people into missions, and destroyed cultures. An action can require bravery but still have harmful consequences. This is why it is important to look at history from multiple perspectives — not just the explorer's, but also the people who were affected. True courage should be paired with justice and respect for others.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Acknowledges that courage and harm can coexist; gives specific examples from exploration; considers multiple perspectives. Developing (2): Says yes or no but reasoning is shallow or lacks examples. Beginning (1): Does not engage with the complexity of the question.
