Learn the Story

Making an Evidence-Based Claim
How historians argue with facts, not opinions

Historians don't just describe what happened — they make claims about why events mattered. A claim is a statement you can defend with evidence. Today, you'll practice this essential historian skill: choosing an event, making a claim about its importance, and backing it up.

What makes a strong claim? A strong claim is specific, arguable, and supported by evidence. "Statehood changed Florida" is too vague. "Statehood in 1845 changed Florida the most because it gave Floridians self-government and tied the state to the national debate over slavery" is specific, arguable, and uses evidence.

What counts as evidence? Evidence includes specific facts, dates, names, numbers, and details from the lessons. "Nearly half of Florida's population was enslaved by 1860" is evidence. "Slavery was bad" is an opinion. Historians build arguments on evidence, not feelings.

The Claim-Evidence-Reasoning pattern. Here's a simple pattern for writing a strong claim:
• Claim: State which event most changed Florida and why.
• Evidence: Give at least two specific facts that support your claim.
• Reasoning: Explain how the evidence connects to your claim — why do these facts prove your point?

Which event will you choose? You could argue for Florida becoming a territory (1821), the Seminole Wars, statehood (1845), the growth of slavery, secession (1861), the Civil War, or Reconstruction. There's no single right answer — what matters is how well you support your claim.

🤔

Pause & Think: Before you write, think: which event has the strongest evidence behind it? Which event caused the most other changes? That's often the strongest claim.
