🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courtesy
Studying another community's way of life calls for courtesy. That means using each people's real name, taking their choices seriously, and not lumping every Native group together. Courtesy isn't just politeness — for a historian, it's a habit of careful, respectful attention.

Two Peoples, Side by Side
A careful comparison: Timucua and Calusa

Yesterday we met four of Florida's first peoples on a map. Today we slow down and really look at two of them — the Timucua and the Calusa. Both lived in Florida. Both lived here for thousands of years. But almost everything about their daily lives was different — because their land was different.

Food. The Timucua hunted deer in the inland forests, fished the freshwater rivers, gathered wild plants, and grew gardens of corn, beans, and squash. The Calusa, surrounded by saltwater and mangroves, ate mostly fish, oysters, clams, and conch. They didn't farm much at all — the sea gave them more than enough.

Shelter. Timucua villages had round houses built from poles, palm thatch, and bark, often arranged inside a tall wooden fence called a palisade. Calusa villages sat on islands the people themselves had slowly built, layer by layer, from shells. Houses there were built on raised shell platforms above the water.

Community. Both peoples had leaders, ceremonies, and ways of teaching their young. Timucua leaders were called caciques. Calusa society was unusually powerful — their leaders controlled a wide network of towns and traded across long distances by canoe. Even European visitors in the 1500s wrote about the Calusa as one of the strongest groups they encountered.

So: same Florida, two very different worlds. One built around rivers, fields, and forests. The other built around the open sea.

🤔

Pause & Think: If you were a Calusa child, what would you probably be learning to do well? If you were a Timucua child, what would you probably be learning to do well? Why are your answers different?

Source Study
Timucua Fortified Village (de Bry engraving, 1591)
🖼️ A Theodor de Bry engraving (published 1591) based on a drawing by the French artist Jacques Le Moyne de Morgues, showing the inside of a fortified Timucua village in northeast Florida: a circular wooden palisade with a narrow entrance, round thatched houses arranged inside, a central plaza. Public domain. Available from the Library of Congress, the Florida Memory project (State Archives of Florida), or the New York Public Library Digital Collections.

Save your image as: source-4-03-02-timucua-village.jpg
Primary Source — Engraving · Theodor de Bry after Jacques Le Moyne, "A Fortified Village," published 1591, based on observations in Florida in the 1560s
👁️

What do I see? Look carefully. How many houses can you count inside the palisade? What shape are they? Is the entrance to the village wide or narrow — and what does that suggest about safety?

💭

What does it mean? The houses are clustered together inside a wall, with one narrow way in and out. What does this tell us about how the Timucua lived together — and what they wanted to protect?

⚖️

Why does it matter? This engraving is one of the very few images we have from the 1500s of a Timucua village. But — the European artist had never been to Florida. He worked from someone else's drawing, and he changed things. So we treat this source carefully: it shows us something real, but not everything, and not perfectly.

💡 Key Concept

The Timucua and the Calusa shared a homeland but built very different lives — and we can show those differences using primary sources like a 1591 engraving. Careful comparison, using real evidence, is how a historian-citizen pushes past easy labels.

Signal Check
🧠

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. Which sentence best compares Timucua and Calusa food?
ABoth peoples relied mostly on farming corn and beans.
BThe Timucua hunted, fished, and farmed; the Calusa relied mostly on the sea.
CThe Timucua ate only seafood; the Calusa grew large crops of corn.
Right — that's a careful comparison. Both lived in Florida, but their land gave them different options.
Think about where each people lived. Could the people in the southwest mangroves grow big cornfields? Could the people in the northern forests fish only saltwater?
2. The de Bry engraving from 1591 is a useful source, but historians treat it carefully. Why?
ABecause it is in black and white instead of color.
BBecause no Timucua people ever saw it.
CBecause the European engraver had never been to Florida and may have changed details.
Exactly — the picture shows us something real, but it was made far from Florida by someone who only saw another artist's sketch. Useful, but not perfect.
Think about who made the engraving and where they were when they made it. That tells you a lot about how much to trust it.
3. What does it look like to study another community with courtesy?
AUse each people's real name, take their choices seriously, and don't lump groups together.
BOnly study peoples who left written records.
CDecide which way of life was "best" and rank the others below it.
Yes — courtesy in history is careful, respectful attention to each people on their own terms.
Courtesy isn't just being polite — it's how a historian-citizen pays attention. Which choice respects each people on their own terms?

Case File · The Echoes Beneath
I noticed something while archiving today's engraving. The European artist drew the chief, the warriors, and the houses — but in the corner of the original sketch there's a small figure of a Timucua child carrying a basket, and her name is nowhere in the records. The Calusa shell mound is named after a Spanish explorer who saw it 200 years later, not after the Calusa women who likely carried most of those shells. These voices weren't written down, but they're here. I'm logging them. The pattern is growing.
