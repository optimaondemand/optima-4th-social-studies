What Florida Historians Study
The four ingredients of Florida's story

Florida historians don't just memorize names and dates. They ask careful questions about the past — and they study four big things to answer those questions.

1. People. Who lived in Florida? Historians study the Calusa and Seminole, Spanish missionaries and Black Seminoles, civil rights leaders and modern Floridians. Every story starts with the people who lived it.

2. Places. Where did things happen? Florida is more than a shape on a map. Historians study its coastlines, springs, swamps, and forts — because places shape what people can do.

3. Events. What happened, and when? Historians put events in order so we can see causes and effects. Florida became a U.S. state in 1845, but the story of "how" stretches back hundreds of years before that.

4. Evidence. How do we know? This is the historian's secret weapon. Old photos, maps, letters, artifacts, oral histories — these are the clues that prove what really happened. Without evidence, history is just a guess.

Signal Check

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. Which of these is NOT one of the four things Florida historians study?
APeople who lived in Florida
BThe favorite snacks of every Floridian today
CPlaces where things happened
Right! Florida historians focus on people, places, events, and evidence — the things that help us understand Florida's story. Favorite snacks aren't usually on the list.
Take another look at the four things historians study. Try the choice that feels most random or unrelated to history.
2. Why do historians need evidence?
ATo prove what really happened, not just what people guess
BBecause old objects look cool in a museum
CTo make their books longer
Exactly. Evidence is how historians know instead of guess. Without it, history would just be opinions and rumors.
Think back to the Distorter Alert. What did the Junior Chrono-Historian protocol say evidence is for?
3. A Florida historian sees an old map of St. Augustine from 1671. Which question would a historian most likely ask?
AIs the map pretty?
BWho made this map, when, and what does it show about Florida at that time?
CCan I keep this map?
That's a historian's question — asking who, when, and what does it show turns a map into evidence about people, places, and events.
Remember: historians ask questions about people, places, events, and evidence. Look for the question that does all three.
