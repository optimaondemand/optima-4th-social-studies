🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Service
The first peoples of Florida served their communities every single day — fishing, gathering, building shelters, teaching the youngest, looking after the oldest. Service didn't mean one big act. It meant the steady, ordinary work that kept everyone safe and fed. As you study their lives, notice the many small jobs that made survival possible.

Four Peoples, Four Worlds
Florida before any European map

Imagine standing on a quiet Florida beach about a thousand years ago. There are no roads, no cars, no buildings on the horizon. But there are people here — and not just one group. Long before Spanish ships ever came into view, Florida was home to many different Native peoples, each shaped by the land and water around them.

Up in the northwest panhandle, the Apalachee built farming villages in the rich, hilly land near present-day Tallahassee. They grew corn, beans, and squash in big fields, stored food for the winter, and held important ceremonies in their towns.

Across the north-central and northeast parts of Florida lived the Timucua — actually a group of related peoples who shared a language family. They lived in round, thatched houses inside wooden fences called palisades. They hunted deer, fished the rivers, and gathered wild plants, but also tended gardens of corn and beans.

Down in southwest Florida, along the warm Gulf coast, lived the Calusa. The Calusa were a sea-and-shellfish people. They built their towns on islands they made themselves — gigantic mounds of empty shells piled up over thousands of years. From their dugout canoes, they fished, gathered oysters, and traveled long routes through the mangroves. They did almost no farming; the sea gave them what they needed.

Along the southeast coast near today's Miami lived the Tequesta. Like the Calusa, they relied on the ocean — fish, sea turtles, manatees, and shellfish. They built small villages near rivers that flowed into the bay.

Four peoples. Four very different daily lives. And one big clue connects them all: where you lived in Florida shaped how you lived.

🤔

Pause & Think: The Apalachee in the panhandle farmed big fields. The Calusa in the southwest barely farmed at all. What might explain the difference? (Hint: think about what the land and water near each group could offer.)

1. Which of these peoples lived in the northwest panhandle of Florida and farmed corn, beans, and squash?
ACalusa
BTequesta
CApalachee
Right — the Apalachee built farming villages in the hills near present-day Tallahassee. The rich land and rainfall there were good for big crops.
The clue is "farming" and "northwest panhandle." Which group lived inland in the hills, not along the southern coasts?
2. The Calusa built towering shell mounds along the southwest coast. What does that tell us about how they got most of their food?
AThey were full-time farmers like the Apalachee.
BThey relied heavily on the sea — fish, oysters, and other shellfish.
CThey mostly traded with Europeans for food.
Exactly — mountains of leftover shells are direct evidence of what people ate, day after day, for centuries. The sea was their pantry.
Look at the shells themselves — what kind of animal do shells come from, and where do those animals live?
3. The Distorter says all of Florida's first peoples lived the same way. What's the best historian-citizen response?
AShow evidence that different regions had different ways of living — like farmers in the north and shell-mound builders in the southwest.
BAgree, because it would make the story easier to remember.
CIgnore the signal — it doesn't matter what people think.
Yes — a Junior Chrono-Historian uses specific evidence to set the record straight. Differences matter.
