Learn the Story
📖
The Council's Two Perspectives
Both are true. The council's job is to hold them together.

The Council Chamber is not a courtroom. There is no winner. The question on the wall — what changed after contact? — has two true answers that happened at the same time, to different people. A council's job is to name them both.

1. What arrived. Europeans brought new animals, tools, crops, and trade routes. Spanish maps redrew Florida with claim lines, missions, and forts. The peninsula became part of a larger world — connected to Europe, Africa, and the rest of the Americas.

2. Who paid. The First Peoples paid most of the cost. Diseases the communities had no defense against spread quickly. Whole groups were lost. Others were pushed from their land or forced into mission labor. Languages, leaders, and ways of life that had held for thousands of years were broken in a few generations.

3. Both at once. These two perspectives don't cancel each other out. They are both what happened. The dishonest move is to share only one — usually Perspective A, because it is easier to write.

4. The historian's sentence. An honest sentence about contact in Florida names the change AND the cost in the same breath. That is the kind of sentence the council is looking for.

Communities forced to change or vanish

Connect the Ideas
⚠️

Watch for the easy shortcut: A sentence about Florida after contact that names only Perspective A — a clean "new world arrived" answer — quietly drops the First Peoples from the story. Which piece of evidence from the council table makes that flattened sentence impossible? A Florida historian protocol: an honest council sentence names both what arrived AND who paid. If the second half is missing, the sentence is not done — no matter how good it sounds.

💡 Key Concept

A council sentence is short, but it holds two truths at once: what changed for the land and the newcomers, and what was lost by the people already here. Florida's Episode 1 closes when both are written into the same sentence.
