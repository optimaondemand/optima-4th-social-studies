Section A
Knowledge Check
⏲ ~10 min
1What is the main purpose of Florida’s constitution?
  • A) To list every law that citizens must follow
  • B) To explain the structure, powers, rights, and responsibilities of state government
  • C) To describe the history of Florida from the earliest settlers
  • D) To tell citizens how much they should pay in taxes
Correct Answer: B
Florida’s constitution is the foundational document that establishes the structure of the state government, defines the powers of each branch, and outlines the rights and responsibilities of citizens. It is not a list of every law — individual laws are created by the legislature.
Common Misconceptions
Students who pick A may confuse the constitution with a law book. A constitution sets up the framework for government; it does not list every specific law. Students who pick D may confuse the constitution with the tax code.
2Which branch of Florida government MAKES the laws?
  • A) Executive branch
  • B) Legislative branch
  • C) Judicial branch
  • D) Local branch
Correct Answer: B
The legislative branch — Florida’s Legislature, which includes the Senate and the House of Representatives — is responsible for writing and passing laws. The word “legislative” comes from the same root as “legislation,” meaning laws.
Common Misconceptions
Students often confuse the executive and legislative branches. Some think the Governor makes laws because the Governor is the most visible leader. Clarify that the Governor signs laws but does not write them. “Local branch” (D) is a distractor — there is no branch called the “local branch.”
3Which branch of Florida government ENFORCES the laws?
  • A) Legislative branch
  • B) Judicial branch
  • C) Executive branch
  • D) Constitutional branch
Correct Answer: C
The executive branch, led by the Governor, is responsible for enforcing (carrying out) the laws. The Governor oversees state agencies, law enforcement, and the day-to-day operations of government. “Execute” means to carry out or put into action.
Common Misconceptions
Students may pick B (judicial) because they associate courts with law enforcement. Remind them that courts interpret laws and settle disputes — they do not enforce them. “Constitutional branch” (D) is a distractor; there is no branch by that name.
4Which branch of Florida government INTERPRETS the laws and settles disputes?
  • A) Executive branch
  • B) Legislative branch
  • C) Judicial branch
  • D) Governor’s branch
Correct Answer: C
The judicial branch includes Florida’s courts, from local courts up to the Florida Supreme Court. Judges interpret laws — they decide what laws mean and whether they follow the constitution. They also settle disputes between people, organizations, or the government.
Common Misconceptions
Students who pick D (“Governor’s branch”) may associate the Governor with all government power. Reinforce that each branch has a separate role, and the Governor leads the executive branch, not the courts. “Governor’s branch” is not a real term.
5Why does Florida’s government have three branches instead of one?
  • A) Because three branches can work faster than one
  • B) So no single branch has too much power — checks and balances
  • C) Because the U.S. president told Florida to have three branches
  • D) So each branch can make its own laws
Correct Answer: B
The separation of powers into three branches exists so that no single part of government becomes too powerful. Each branch can “check” the others — for example, the Governor can veto a law, and the courts can rule a law unconstitutional. This system of checks and balances protects citizens’ rights.
Common Misconceptions
Students who pick A may think having more branches means faster work; in reality, the purpose is balance, not speed. Students who pick D misunderstand separation of powers — only the legislative branch makes laws. Option C is factually wrong; the structure comes from the U.S. and Florida constitutions, not a presidential order.
6A pothole on a city street would most likely be fixed by which level of government?
  • A) The federal government in Washington, D.C.
  • B) The state government in Tallahassee
  • C) Local government (city or county)
  • D) The judicial branch
Correct Answer: C
Local governments (cities and counties) are responsible for maintaining local roads, sidewalks, parks, and other community infrastructure. A pothole on a city street is a local issue that would be handled by the city or county public works department, not by the state or federal government.
Common Misconceptions
Students may think the state government handles everything within Florida. Help them understand the difference between local issues (potholes, parks, trash pickup) and state issues (statewide laws, highways, state parks). Option D confuses a branch of government with a level of government.
7What is the difference between income and credit?
  • A) Income and credit are the same thing
  • B) Income is money you earn; credit is money you borrow and must pay back
  • C) Income is money you borrow; credit is money you save
  • D) Income is only for adults; credit is only for children
Correct Answer: B
Income is money that a person earns through work, such as a paycheck, allowance, or selling goods. Credit is money borrowed from a bank or lender that must be paid back, usually with interest. Understanding this difference is a key financial literacy concept.
Common Misconceptions
Students may confuse “credit” with “savings” or think income and credit are the same because both involve money. Emphasize that credit is borrowed money with an obligation to repay, while income is money you have earned and do not owe back. Option C reverses the definitions.
8Which of these is an example of a financial RISK?
  • A) Putting part of your allowance into a savings account each week
  • B) Making a budget before you go shopping
  • C) Spending all your savings on something you might not need
  • D) Comparing prices before buying something
Correct Answer: C
A financial risk is a decision that could lead to losing money or being worse off financially. Spending all your savings on something you might not need is risky because you could end up with no money left for things you actually need. Options A, B, and D are all examples of responsible financial habits.
Common Misconceptions
Students may not clearly understand what “risk” means in a financial context. Help them see that risk involves the chance of losing money or making a poor decision. The other options (saving, budgeting, comparing prices) are all strategies to reduce risk, not examples of risk itself.

Diagram: Florida’s Three Branches of Government

Study the diagram below showing Florida’s three branches of government and one key responsibility for each branch.

Legislative Branch — Makes the laws. The Florida Legislature includes the Senate and the House of Representatives.

Executive Branch — Enforces the laws. Led by the Governor of Florida, who signs bills into law and oversees state agencies.

Judicial Branch — Interprets the laws. Florida’s courts, including the Supreme Court, settle disputes and decide whether laws follow the constitution.
9Use the diagram above to answer the following question.
A new law about protecting Florida’s beaches has been proposed. Explain the steps: Which branch writes it? Which branch signs it into law? Which branch would decide if it follows the constitution?
Model Response (Secure — 3)
If a new law about protecting Florida’s beaches is proposed, here is how the three branches would be involved. First, the legislative branch would write the law. Members of the Florida Senate or House of Representatives would draft a bill about beach protection and vote on it. If it passes, the bill goes to the executive branch. The Governor would review the bill and decide whether to sign it into law. If the Governor signs it, the law takes effect. Later, if someone challenges the law — for example, if a business says the law is unfair — the judicial branch would step in. A court would interpret the law and decide whether it follows the Florida Constitution. This is how the three branches work together: one writes, one enforces, and one checks.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Correctly identifies all three branches and their roles in the process (legislative writes, executive signs/enforces, judicial interprets/checks constitutionality). Uses details from the diagram. Developing (2): Gets two of the three branches correct or describes the process but mixes up one role. Beginning (1): Only identifies one branch correctly or gives a vague answer without connecting to the diagram.
10Think about what you learned about local and state government.
A family in your community wants a new park built. Would they go to local government or state government? Explain why.
Model Response (Secure — 3)
The family would go to local government, like the city council or county commission, to ask for a new park. A neighborhood park is a local issue because it affects one community, not the whole state. Local governments are responsible for things like parks, playgrounds, sidewalks, and community buildings in their area. The family could attend a town hall or city council meeting to share their idea, or they could contact their local representative. State government in Tallahassee deals with bigger issues that affect all of Florida, like statewide highways or state parks. Since this family wants a park in their own neighborhood, local government is the right place to go.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Correctly identifies local government, explains why (local issue, community-level), and may mention how citizens participate (town hall, contacting representatives). Developing (2): Says local government but explanation of why is vague or incomplete. Beginning (1): Says state government, or says local government with no explanation. Note: Accept if a student argues state government for a state park, as long as reasoning is sound.

These questions ask students to think carefully and write multi-sentence responses. Look for specific details from Unit 12 and clear reasoning.

11Read the prompt below.
Why is it important for citizens — even young people — to understand how government works? Use one example from this unit to support your answer.
Model Response (Secure — 3)
It is important for citizens — even young people — to understand how government works because government makes decisions that affect everyone’s daily life. When citizens understand the three branches of government, they know where to go when they want something to change. For example, in Unit 12 we learned that citizens can attend town hall meetings or contact their local representatives to solve community problems, like getting a new crosswalk or fixing a broken playground. If you do not understand how government works, you might not know that you have the power to speak up and make a difference. Even young people can start learning now so that when they are old enough to vote, they will make informed choices. Understanding government is part of being a responsible citizen.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Clearly explains why understanding government matters, gives a specific example from Unit 12, and connects to citizenship or participation. Developing (2): Says it is important but the example is vague or only partially connected to Unit 12. Beginning (1): One sentence with no example, or restates the question. Accept any valid Unit 12 example: three branches, checks and balances, local vs. state government, citizen participation at town halls, voting, contacting representatives.
12Read the prompt below.
Imagine you receive $20 for your birthday. Describe how you would divide it among saving, spending, and maybe donating. Explain your choices using what you learned about responsible money decisions.
Model Response (Secure — 3)
If I received $20 for my birthday, I would divide it into three parts. First, I would save $10 by putting it in my savings account. Saving is important because it gives you money for the future in case you need it for something important, like school supplies or an emergency. Next, I would spend $5 on something I have been wanting, like a book or a small game. I would make sure to compare prices first so I get the best deal. Finally, I would donate $5 to a cause I care about, like a local animal shelter. In Unit 12, we learned that being responsible with money means thinking carefully before you spend, saving for the future, and not taking unnecessary risks. By dividing my money this way, I am making a plan instead of spending it all at once, which would be a financial risk.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Describes a clear plan for dividing money, explains reasoning for each choice, and connects to financial literacy concepts from Unit 12 (saving, budgeting, risk, responsibility). Developing (2): Divides the money but explanation is vague or does not connect to Unit 12 concepts. Beginning (1): Says “I would save some and spend some” with no reasoning or amounts. Note: There is no single “right” split — accept any reasonable division as long as the student explains their reasoning. Donating is optional (the prompt says “maybe donating”).

Virtue Lens Reflection
⏲ ~5 min
⚖️ Virtue Focus: Responsibility
Responsibility means taking care of your duties and being accountable for your choices. In government and in personal finances, responsibility helps people and communities thrive.
13Reflect on what you learned in Unit 12.
Think about one of the virtues from this unit: responsibility or self-governance. Choose a person or situation from our government and economy lessons and explain how that virtue was important.
Model Response (Secure — 3)
Responsibility was an important virtue in our lesson about Florida’s three branches of government. Each branch has a specific responsibility — the legislative branch is responsible for making laws, the executive branch is responsible for enforcing them, and the judicial branch is responsible for interpreting them. If any branch failed to do its job responsibly, the whole system would break down. For example, if the judicial branch did not carefully check whether laws follow the constitution, citizens’ rights could be taken away. Responsibility also showed up in our financial literacy lesson. When a person makes a budget and saves money instead of spending everything at once, they are showing self-governance — they are controlling their choices instead of acting on impulse. Responsibility matters because it keeps government fair and helps individuals make smart decisions with their money.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Names responsibility or self-governance, connects it to a specific example from Unit 12 (government branches, financial decisions, citizen participation), and explains why the virtue matters. Developing (2): Names the virtue and gives an example but the explanation of why it matters is vague. Beginning (1): No specific example, or no connection to the virtue. Accept any valid example: branches of government fulfilling their duties, citizens participating responsibly, making responsible financial choices, self-governance in budgeting or saving.
