🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Honesty
Honesty is the historian's first rule. When a Florida historian says "I know what happened," that claim has to come from real evidence — and the historian has to be honest about where the evidence came from. A diary written by a Seminole leader in 1830 and a textbook chapter written in 2010 both tell us something. Pretending they're the same kind of source would be dishonest. Today, you'll practice naming sources for what they really are.

🔎 Discovery Fact
When historians say "primary source," they literally mean evidence that comes first — straight from the moment it happened. That's the same prim- hiding inside "prime time" and "prime minister."

Primary & Secondary Sources
Two voices, two jobs in a historian's evidence box

Yesterday we learned that Florida historians study people, places, events, and evidence. Today we zoom in on that last one — evidence — because not all evidence is the same. Florida historians sort evidence into two big groups: primary sources and secondary sources.

Primary sources are evidence from someone who was there at the time. Think of a primary source as a voice from inside the moment. Examples from Florida history:

  • A 1565 letter Pedro Menéndez de Avilés sent to the King of Spain about founding St. Augustine
  • A photograph of workers building Henry Flagler's railroad across Florida in the 1890s
  • An oral history recorded from a Seminole elder remembering their grandparents' stories
  • A diary kept by a Florida citrus farmer in the 1880s

Secondary sources are accounts made later by people who studied the primary sources. Think of a secondary source as a voice from outside the moment, looking back. Examples:

  • A textbook chapter about St. Augustine, written in 2010
  • A documentary film about the Trail of Tears, made in this century
  • A museum panel that explains how Florida became a state in 1845
  • This lesson! It's a secondary source telling you about Florida history.

Both kinds matter. A primary source brings you close to the moment but only shows one person's view. A secondary source pulls together many primary sources but adds a step between you and the past. A good Florida historian uses both — and is honest about which is which.

1. In 1565, Pedro Menéndez de Avilés wrote a letter to the King of Spain about founding St. Augustine. What kind of source is that letter today?
AA secondary source, because it's really old
BA primary source, because it came from someone who was there at the time
CNeither — letters don't count as sources
Right. The letter came from someone who was there at the time, so it's a primary source. Age alone doesn't decide it — what matters is whether the source is from inside the moment.
Re-read the definition of primary source. The question isn't how old the source is — it's whether the person who made it was there at the time.
2. Which of these is a secondary source about the Seminole people?
AA diary entry written by a Seminole leader in 1830
BA textbook chapter about the Seminole written in 2010
CA photograph taken inside a Seminole village in 1900
Yes! The textbook was written later, by an author who studied other sources. That's a secondary source. The diary and the photograph both came from inside the moment, so they're primary.
Look for the source that was made later, by someone studying the past — not by someone who lived it.
3. Why does it matter to tell primary and secondary sources apart?
APrimary sources are always right and secondary sources are always wrong
BWe have to be honest about where evidence came from so we can judge what it really shows
CSecondary sources don't count for history
Exactly. Both kinds of sources help us — but only if we're honest about which is which. That honesty is what protects the truth.
