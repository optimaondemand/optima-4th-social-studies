Learn the Story
📖
What European Colonization Caused — and Left Behind
Looking back at Unit 4 as one whole picture

Over four lessons, we've gathered the pieces of Unit 4. Today we put them together. Why did Europeans come, and what did their arrival cause?

The causes are the parts of the story we've already studied: the motives of God, Gold, and Glory; the rivalry between Spain, France, and England; and the long search for new lands, resources, and routes. These motives kept ships sailing across the ocean for more than 200 years.

The effects are harder to summarize, because some were good for some people and devastating for others. The biggest effect was the spread of disease. European ships brought illnesses like smallpox and measles — diseases the First Peoples of Florida had never been exposed to. Tens of thousands of people died in a single generation. Whole villages were lost. The Calusa, Timucua, Apalachee, and Tequesta nations slowly disappeared, not because they weren't strong, but because biological forces no one fully understood at the time tore through their communities.

There were other effects too. The Spanish built dozens of missions — small communities where priests taught Christianity and the Spanish language. Some Native people accepted the missions; many resisted. The Spanish also built St. Augustine, the oldest continuously occupied European settlement in the United States — still here today. New crops, new animals (like horses, pigs, and cattle), and new tools traveled in both directions across the ocean. And finally, after 200 years of Spanish control, England took Florida in 1763 — the flags changed again.

An honest historian holds both halves of the picture. Courage and cost. Faith and force. Settlement and loss. Florida today exists because of all of these effects — and remembering them clearly is the work of a Florida historian.

👁️

What do I see? Look at the page carefully. What kinds of marks do you see — names? dates? crosses? Are there many entries or few? Is the handwriting careful or rushed?

💭

What does it mean? A book like this was kept by Spanish priests at a mission. Many entries are baptisms (new members of the church). Others are burials (people who died). What can the rhythm of those two kinds of entries tell us about life at the mission?

⚖️

Why does it matter? Why does this kind of record matter so much to historians studying the effects of European colonization on Florida? What can a counted name tell us that a story cannot?
