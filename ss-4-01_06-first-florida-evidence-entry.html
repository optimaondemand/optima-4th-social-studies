Today honesty returns — and it's the heart of the whole entry. An honest Florida historian makes a claim they can back up with evidence. A Historian's Notebook entry that says "I think X because of evidence A and B — and I'm still wondering about C" is stronger than one that pretends to have all the answers. Learn the Story
📖
Writing Your First Historian's Notebook Entry
Claim · Evidence · One Honest Wondering

A Historian's Notebook entry isn't a guess. It's not a long story either. It's a short, careful piece of writing with three parts. Once you know the parts, you can write one any day.

1. The claim. One clear sentence that says what you think is true. Use historian language — "I think…," "The evidence shows…," "This source helps me understand…" The claim isn't a wild guess. It's something you can back up.

2. The evidence. At least two specific details from a Florida source that support your claim. Don't write "it just feels true." Write what you can see in the source — a date, a name, a place, an object in a photo, a word in a document.

3. One honest wondering. A real question you can't answer yet. This is where honesty shows up most. Honest historians don't pretend to know the whole story. They name what they're still wondering, and that makes the entry stronger — not weaker.

Here's how the three parts sound when they're stitched together:

📓

"I think the people in this Florida photograph worked hard at a difficult job. The evidence shows their work clothes are well-worn and the tools are heavy. I'm still wondering who they were and how they were paid for this work."

Three sentences. Claim. Evidence. Honest question. That's a Historian's Notebook entry.

Signal Check

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. Which of these is the best example of a historian's claim?
AThis photograph is awesome.
BI think the people in this photograph worked hard at a difficult job.
CI don't know anything about this photograph.
Right. A claim says what you think is true — clearly enough that someone could ask "what's your evidence?" "Awesome" doesn't make a claim; "I don't know" gives up. Option B is a real claim a historian can back up.
A claim is a clear "I think…" sentence. Not a reaction word, not a refusal — a real statement someone could check.
2. A Junior Chrono-Historian writes: "I think these workers had a hard job. The evidence shows their clothes are worn and the tools are heavy." What's missing from a complete Historian's Notebook entry?
ANothing — it's done
BMore claims — at least three
COne honest wondering — a question the source can't answer on its own
Exactly. The claim is there, the evidence is there, but the entry is missing the third part: an honest wondering. That's what makes the entry trustworthy.
Re-read the Key Concept. A complete entry has three parts: claim, evidence, and an honest question you can't yet answer.

Yes. An honest "I'm still wondering…" tells the reader where the source stops and where more research has to begin. That makes the entry more trustworthy, not less.
