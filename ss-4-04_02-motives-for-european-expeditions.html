Yesterday we said the Spanish explorers had courage. Today we ask a harder question: was their courage used for something good? A Virtue Lens helps a historian look at the same action two ways — what it took to do it, and what it cost the people on the other end. Both halves of that picture belong in the record.

Learn the Story

Why Europeans Sailed to Florida — God, Gold, and Glory
The three motives behind first contact

When historians study why the Spanish sailed across the ocean to Florida, they usually point to three big reasons. They are easy to remember because they all start with G: God, Gold, and Glory.

God meant religion. Spain was a Catholic country, and the Spanish king believed it was his job to spread the Catholic faith to people in other lands. Priests traveled with the explorers and later built churches called missions. They wanted to convert Native peoples — the Calusa, Timucua, Apalachee, and Tequesta — to Christianity. Some Native people accepted, but many resisted, because they had their own beliefs and their own way of life.

Gold meant wealth. The Spanish had already found enormous amounts of silver and gold in places like Mexico and Peru, and they hoped Florida would hide the same kind of treasure. That is why de Soto marched so far and pushed his men so hard — he was searching for cities of gold that he had only heard rumors about. The cities did not exist, but the Spanish kept searching for decades.

Glory meant honor for Spain and rewards for the explorer himself. If an explorer claimed a new land for the king, he could be given a noble title, land of his own, and a share of any wealth the land produced. Spain was also racing against France and England — every country wanted to plant its flag first.

These three motives are why expedition after expedition kept coming, even after disasters like Narváez's. They are also why first contact in Florida was so often violent. The Spanish were not visiting — they were claiming, converting, and searching, sometimes by force.
