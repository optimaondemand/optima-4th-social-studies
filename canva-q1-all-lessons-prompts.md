## THE MUSEUM STORY

The students are **Junior Curators** at a living history museum called **The Optima Museum of Human Experience**. The museum has three wings that open across Q1 — each wing is a new unit. Within each wing, each lesson is a new room or exhibit that wasn't accessible before.

The museum has a personality. It responds to what the students learn. Rooms stay locked until the students prove they're ready. Display cases are covered until the lesson reveals them. The Chief Curator leaves memos. Doors open. Badges are earned.

This is not a metaphor. The museum *is the curriculum*. Teach it like it's real.

**Classical & Logic Pedagogy Principles (woven throughout):**
- Every lesson opens with **observation before interpretation** — students describe before they conclude
- Every lesson has a **Socratic question** — one question with no single right answer, worth sitting with
- Every lesson practices the **claim → evidence → reasoning** chain (at 3rd grade level: "I think ___ because I noticed ___")
- Every lesson closes with a **copywork/chronicle** moment — a sentence or entry that students write and keep
- The **trivium** maps to the units: Unit 1 = Grammar (the facts and vocabulary of history), Unit 2 = Logic (applying evidence to claims), Unit 3 = Rhetoric (communicating about place and direction)

> - 🟩 **GREEN (#2E7D4F) = "Raise Your Hand"** — Students use the Teams Raise Hand feature. Used when you want one or two students to speak.
> - 🟥 **TERRACOTTA (#C7522A) = "In Your Groups"** — Students go into Teams Breakout Rooms. Used for collaborative reasoning tasks (3–5 minutes).
>
> Every action box must have: (1) a bold header label naming the interaction type, (2) a thin divider line, (3) the student-facing prompt in body text beneath. NO other interaction types (no polls, no annotation, no whiteboard).
>
> **LIVE CLASS STRUCTURE — applies to every deck:**
> This is a 45-minute live class. The slide order must follow this arc:
> - Slides 1–3: **Open** — arrive at the museum, enter this lesson's gallery, ask the big question
> - Slides 4–7: **Build** — teach the vocabulary and concepts the exit ticket requires
> - Slides 8–12: **Apply** — use the vocabulary on real sources or problems, with interaction at every 2nd or 3rd slide
> - Slides 13–15: **Close** — independent task handoff, exit ticket, forward look (badge or next lesson)
>
> Every 2–3 slides must include an action box. No student goes 10 minutes without being asked to think out loud.
>
> **BACKWARD PLANNING RULE:**
> Every slide earns its place by answering one question: does this slide give students something they need to complete the exit ticket? If yes, keep it. If no, cut it. The exit ticket is specified in each lesson prompt.
>
> **SPEAKER NOTES:**
> Add speaker notes to every slide. Speaker notes are teacher-facing only. They must include: (1) what the teacher says or asks, (2) what a strong student response sounds like, and (3) how to transition to the next slide.
>
> **CLASSICAL PEDAGOGY:**
> Every lesson must include at least one of the following classical elements:
> - A **Socratic question** (no single right answer — worth 3+ minutes of discussion)
> - A **word root** moment (a Latin or Greek root that unlocks meaning)
> - An **observation → inference → claim** chain (students describe → interpret → conclude)
> - A **copywork sentence** (a sentence students write or type that is worth keeping)

## LESSON 3.01.02 — Primary vs. Secondary Sources (Deep Dive)
**Week 1 · Virtue: Honesty · Routine: Source Study**

### Museum Story Moment
*The first display case is uncovered. Inside: a primary source — a letter written by hand. But there's also a textbook open to a chapter about the same event. The Chief Curator asks: "Both of these tell the same story. Are they the same kind of evidence?"*

### Exit Ticket
Students classify 5 sources as Primary or Secondary and write one sentence explaining the most important difference.

### Classical Element
**The grammar of historical thinking.** Students learn the rule (primary = at the time, secondary = later) and then apply it to increasingly tricky cases. Socratic question: *"Can a secondary source ever be more trustworthy than a primary source?"* (Yes — a historian who studied 100 primary sources may be more accurate than one eyewitness.)

## LESSON 3.01.03 — Reading a Historical Source (SIFT)
**Week 2 · Virtue: Honesty · Routine: Source Study**

### Museum Story Moment
*A suspicious source arrives by courier — an anonymous letter claiming to describe an ancient battle. It is not signed. The date is smudged. The Chief Curator refuses to add it to the archive until it passes inspection. Students must learn and apply the SIFT method.*

### Exit Ticket
Apply SIFT (Stop, Investigate the Source, Find Better Coverage, Trace Claims) to the anonymous letter. Write: "I (trust / do not trust) this source because ___."

### Classical Element
**The logic of source evaluation.** SIFT is a formal method — a logical procedure. Students learn that skepticism is not cynicism: asking "who made this?" is not rude; it is the most important question a historian asks. Socratic question: *"Is it possible to be too skeptical of sources?"*

### Lesson Prompt for Canva AI

> **Lesson 3.01.03 — "Reading a Historical Source (SIFT)"**
>
> **Accent color this lesson:** TEAL (#1A8A7D)
>
> **Story moment:** A locked courier case arrives at the museum entrance. Inside: an unsigned letter describing an ancient battle. No date. No signature. The Chief Curator places it under glass and says: "We don't add anything to this museum without running it through the filter. Today you learn the filter."
>
> **Slide 1 (Title):** "Reading a Historical Source" — show the courier case in a spotlight display case as the hero visual.
>
> **Slide 2 (Story/Hook):** The unsigned letter displayed in a glass case. Handwritten on aged parchment. Chat: "Just looking at it — before reading a word — what questions do you have about this source?"
>
> **Slides 4–7 (Build — SIFT Method):** One slide per letter of SIFT. Each slide is a single framed display case card:
> - S = STOP. Don't share before you think. Ask: "What is my first reaction — and why?"
> - I = INVESTIGATE the Source. Who made this? When? What were their motives?
> - F = FIND Better Coverage. Is there another source that confirms or denies this?
> - T = TRACE Claims. Where did this information originally come from?
>
> **Slide 8 (Apply — SIFT the Letter):** Show the anonymous letter. Groups: "Run this letter through all four SIFT steps. What do you find?"
>
> **Slide 10 (Socratic):** "Is it possible to be TOO skeptical of sources? If we questioned every source, would we ever know anything?" Raise Hand — make the case for YES, then for NO.
>
> **Exit ticket (slide 14):** "I (trust / do not trust) this source because ___. The most important SIFT step for this source was ___ because ___."

## LESSON 3.01.04 — Technology as a Research Tool

### Classical Element
**Applying logic to a new medium.** The same skepticism students learned in 3.01.03 applies to digital sources. The extension: digital sources can be updated, copied, and taken out of context in ways physical sources cannot. Socratic question: *"If everyone can publish online, does that mean no one is an expert anymore?"*

## LESSON 3.01.05 — Latin and Greek Roots (hist-, geo-, eco-, civ-)
**Week 3 · Virtue: Honesty · Routine: Word Root Study**

### Museum Story Moment
*A locked door at the end of the Training Hall is finally opened. Inside: the Language Archive — the oldest room in the museum. The walls are covered in fragments of ancient Greek and Latin text. The Chief Curator says: "Every word in social studies has ancestors. If you know the ancestors, you can decode any word you meet."*

### Exit Ticket
Given 3 unfamiliar words containing known roots, students decode the meaning and write a definition using the root's meaning as their evidence.

### Classical Element
**Etymology as grammar.** This is a classical grammar-stage skill — learning the "atoms" of language. The four roots: *hist-* (knowledge, inquiry), *geo-* (earth), *eco-* (household/environment), *civ-* (citizen). Socratic question: *"If the root of 'history' means 'inquiry' — not just the past — what does that tell us about what historians are really supposed to do?"*

### Lesson Prompt for Canva AI

> **Lesson 3.01.05 — "Latin and Greek Roots"**
>
> **Accent color this lesson:** MOSS (#2E7D4F)
>
> **Story moment:** A locked door in the Training Hall opens for the first time. Inside: the Language Archive. Ancient Greek and Latin fragments cover the walls. The display cases hold not objects but words — carved in stone, written on parchment, etched in copper. Chat opener: "Look at these words on the wall: historian, geography, economics, civics. What do they have in common?"
>
> **Slides 4–7 (Build — one root per slide):** Each slide is a stone tablet or carved inscription visual showing the root:
> - HIST- (Greek: histor = one who knows, one who inquires) → history, historian, prehistoric
> - GEO- (Greek: gē = earth) → geography, geology, geopolitics
> - ECO- (Greek: oikos = household, environment) → economics, ecology, ecosystem
> - CIV- (Latin: civis = citizen) → civics, civil, civilization, civilian
>
> **Slide 8 (Apply — Decode New Words):** Show 6 unfamiliar words. Groups decode using roots: "historiography," "geothermal," "ecosystem," "incivility." Students use the root to build a definition before looking it up.
>
> **Slide 10 (Socratic):** "The Greek root of 'history' — histor — means 'one who inquires,' not just 'the past.' What does that tell us about what historians are really supposed to do?" Raise Hand.
>
> **Slide 12 (Copywork moment):** Students copy one sentence into their Curator's Chronicle: "The word ___ comes from the root ___, which means ___. That tells me that ___ is really about ___."
>
> **Exit ticket (slide 14):** Given three words: "geocentric," "economic," "prehistoric." Students write a definition for each using root knowledge only. "I know ___ means ___ because the root ___ means ___."

## LESSON 3.01.06 — The Evidence Entry (Unit 1 Capstone)
**Week 3 · Virtue: Honesty · Routine: Curator's Chronicle**

### Museum Story Moment
*The Training Hall is fully lit for the first time. Every display case is uncovered. Every door is open. Now the Chief Curator gives the final test: students must write a complete evidence entry — claim, evidence, reasoning — about one source they've studied this unit. If the entry is honest and complete, the Evidence Keeper badge is awarded.*

### Exit Ticket
A complete Curator's Chronicle evidence entry with three parts: (1) What the source is and what type it is, (2) One specific thing it tells us (with a detail from the source), (3) One thing it cannot tell us — and why.

### Classical Element
**Rhetoric — the complete argument.** This is the unit's culmination: students write a structured three-part entry that mirrors classical rhetorical structure (claim → evidence → qualification). The qualification (what the source cannot tell us) is the honesty component — no source is perfect, and saying so is not weakness; it is precision. Socratic question: *"Is an incomplete evidence entry better than no entry at all?"*

### Lesson Prompt for Canva AI

> **Lesson 3.01.06 — "The Evidence Entry — Unit 1 Capstone"**
>
> **Accent color this lesson:** GOLD (#C8A84B) — this is the badge lesson; gold is dominant.
>
> **Story moment:** Every display case in the Training Hall is uncovered tonight. The room is fully illuminated. The Chief Curator stands at the front and says: "You have learned what historians use. You have learned how to read a source. You have learned the language. Now you write your first real evidence entry." The Evidence Keeper badge sits in a spotlight case at the front of the room.
>
> **Slide 1 (Title):** "The Evidence Entry — Unit 1 Capstone." The badge in a spotlight case is the title slide's hero visual.
>
> **Slide 3 (Socratic open):** "Is an incomplete evidence entry better than no entry at all?" Raise Hand — both sides. Then: "Historians say: an honest incomplete entry is always better than a confident wrong one. The qualification isn't a weakness — it's the proof you looked carefully."
>
> **Slides 4–7 (Build — the three-part entry):** Three-slide structure showing the anatomy of an evidence entry. Use a clipboard or parchment card visual:
> - Part 1: SOURCE IDENTIFICATION — What is it? What type? Who made it? When?
> - Part 2: WHAT IT TELLS US — One specific claim supported by one specific detail from the source
> - Part 3: WHAT IT CANNOT TELL US — One honest limitation + why
>
> **Slide 8 (Model Entry — teacher does one together):** Full model entry displayed on parchment. Teacher reads it aloud. Groups: "Is this entry honest? Is anything missing?"
>
> **Slide 10 (Students write):** Copywork moment — students complete their own entry in the Curator's Chronicle for a source they choose from Unit 1. Chat: "Type your Part 2 sentence — just that one sentence — in the chat." Teacher reads 3–4 aloud.
>
> **Slide 12 (Peer review):** Groups: exchange entries. Is Part 3 (the limitation) present? Is it honest? Groups report: "Our partner's entry was honest because ___."
>
> **Slide 14 (Exit ticket + Badge):** Exit ticket is the completed evidence entry. Badge slide: "If your chronicle entry has all three parts and your Part 3 names one honest limitation — you have earned the Evidence Keeper badge."

## LESSON 3.02.01 — Why People Explore (Push/Pull, 3 Gs)
**Week 4 · Virtue: Courage · Routine: Enter the Scene**

### Museum Story Moment
*The Vault door opens for the first time. The first exhibit: a world map with three glowing symbols — a gold coin, a crown, and a cross. Three reasons people left everything they knew. But a question mark hangs over the map: why would anyone do this?*

### Exit Ticket
Identify the push factor AND pull factor for a given historical explorer. Name at least one of the 3 Gs that motivated them. Write: "_____ explored because _____ pushed them away from home and _____ pulled them toward the unknown."

### Classical Element

# THE STORYBOOK — Q1 MUSEUM ARC IN ONE PAGE
# ══════════════════════════════════════════════

*For reference, for narration, for the teacher who wants to feel the whole arc before teaching it.*

**Week 1:** The museum opens. The Training Hall is dark and full of covered cases. Junior Curators arrive not knowing what historians do. By the end of the week, the first case is uncovered — a handwritten letter. They can name it. They can classify it. They are beginning.

**Week 2:** The Training Hall's second room opens: the Skeptic's Room. A suspicious source arrives — an anonymous letter. Students learn to question before they accept. They learn that the internet is a library and a rumor mill and you need to know the difference. They are becoming careful.

**Week 3:** The Language Archive. The oldest room in the museum. Words carved in stone. Students discover that the vocabulary of social studies is not arbitrary — it has roots, and the roots tell you what the subject is really about. Inquiry. Earth. Household. Citizen. They write their first full evidence entry. The Training Hall is fully lit. The Evidence Keeper badge is awarded.

**Week 4:** A door with a brass anchor. The Explorer's Vault opens. Salt water and old wood. Three glowing symbols on the wall: a coin, a crown, a cross. Students ask the hardest question first: why would anyone leave everything they knew? By the end of the week, they are looking at portraits — and looking behind the portraits.

**Week 5:** The Navigation Room. Instruments under glass that crossed oceans. The Exchange Display — two cases, one for each direction, and one dark slide for what can't be put in a case. Students begin to understand that the Columbian Exchange was not symmetrical. They begin to understand what courage actually costs.

**Week 6:** The Skeptic's Corner. One display case holds Columbus's journal. The case next to it is empty. Students learn that absence of evidence is itself evidence — and that the empty case is the most honest thing in the Vault. They fill the Explorer Evidence Chart. Every column. Including the hard one. The Navigator's Compass is awarded.

**Week 7:** The Cartography Gallery. A puzzle room. Maps with covered labels. A damaged compass rose on an antique floor map. Students learn that a map is not a picture of the world — it is an argument about it. They learn that North is North whether someone draws it or not. They are becoming precise.

**Week 8:** The mystery map. Two cities that look close. A scale bar that reveals they are 250 miles apart. The story of the soldiers who died because no one checked. Students plan an expedition. They calculate every distance. The Gallery closes. Three badges sit side by side. Q1 is complete.

*And the museum asks its one question — the question it has been asking all quarter:*

**How do we know what we know — and how do we prove it honestly?**
