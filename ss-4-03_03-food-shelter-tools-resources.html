The Four Solutions
How Florida’s first peoples built lives that worked

Every community on Earth has to solve the same four problems: get food, build shelter, make tools, and gather the resources you need from the land around you. Florida’s first peoples solved all four — and they did it without grocery stores, hardware stores, or any of the things we use today. They had to know their environment so well that the environment became the store.

Food. Different peoples used different food strategies depending on where they lived. The Apalachee in the north grew big fields of corn, beans, and squash — three crops that grow well together. The Timucua hunted deer in the woods, fished the freshwater rivers, and kept smaller gardens. The Calusa and Tequesta got most of their food from the sea: fish, oysters, conch, sea turtle, and manatee. To make food last past the day they caught it, they smoked fish on wooden racks and stored corn and seeds in baskets and pots.

Shelter. One of the smartest Florida shelters is the chickee — a raised platform with a steep palm-thatch roof and no walls. The raised floor kept people above floodwater and snakes. The thatch let warm air rise out so the house stayed cool in summer. The open sides let breezes pass through. Different groups built different versions of houses — round thatched houses in Timucua villages, shell-platform houses among the Calusa — but every choice was a response to the weather and the land.

Tools. Florida’s first peoples were expert toolmakers. They chipped chert (a hard stone) into sharp spear points, knives, and scrapers. They carved shells into hammers, gouges, and fishhooks. They made dugout canoes by burning and scraping out the inside of a single huge cypress log — some Calusa canoes were over 20 feet long. They wove palmetto fiber into baskets and rope. They shaped clay into pots for cooking and storage.

Resources. Almost everything the first peoples used came from within a few miles of home: cypress and pine wood, palmetto leaves, deerskin, bird feathers, shell, chert, clay, freshwater, saltwater, plants for medicine. Knowing which plant, which stone, and when to gather it took years of learning passed from elders to children. That knowledge was its own kind of treasure.

Quick Self-Check
🧠

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. A chickee shelter has a raised floor, a steep palm-thatch roof, and open sides. What problem is each part solving?
AThe raised floor is decoration; the open sides keep enemies out.
BThe raised floor keeps people above floodwater and animals; the thatch lets hot air rise; the open sides let breezes cool the inside.
CEach part is random — there’s no reason for any of it.
Right — every part of a chickee is a smart answer to Florida’s weather and water.
Think about Florida’s weather. Hot, humid, lots of rain and flooding. Which features would help with that?
2. The Calusa carved long dugout canoes out of cypress logs. What does this tool tell us about how they lived?
AThey mostly traveled by walking long distances overland.
BThey didn’t use the water at all.
CWater travel and fishing were a huge part of their daily lives.
Exactly — a canoe is built for water. Calusa life happened on and around the water.
A tool tells you what a community spent its time doing. Where would you use a canoe?
3. Some people call the first peoples’ lives "simple." What’s the most honest response?
A"Simple" is the wrong word. Their lives took deep knowledge of plants, animals, weather, and toolmaking that took years to learn.
B"Simple" is the right word because they didn’t have electricity.
CIt doesn’t matter what word we use.
Yes — honesty means picking words that match the evidence. The evidence shows real skill.
Honesty in history means the words have to match the evidence. What does the evidence show?
