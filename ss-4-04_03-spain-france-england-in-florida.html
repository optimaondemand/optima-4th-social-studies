Courage took different shapes in this story. Spanish soldiers showed courage building a settlement at St. Augustine. French Huguenot settlers showed courage trying to start a new home at Fort Caroline. And the Timucua and Apalachee showed courage just by holding on to who they were while three foreign powers fought across their land. A historian sees all three.

Learn the Story

Three Powers, One Florida Coast
1564 · 1565 · 1586 · and beyond

For most of the 1500s, Spain treated Florida like it belonged to Spain. But Spain wasn't the only country with ships. France and England wanted Florida too — for the same reasons we studied yesterday: God, Gold, and Glory.

In 1564, French Protestants called Huguenots built Fort Caroline near what is now Jacksonville. They were hoping to find a safe place to live and practice their faith. But Spain saw the French fort as a threat. One year later, in 1565, the Spanish admiral Pedro Menéndez de Avilés founded St. Augustine — the oldest continuously occupied European settlement in what is now the United States. Menéndez then attacked Fort Caroline and killed many of the French settlers at a place called Matanzas Inlet (the Spanish word matanzas means "killings").

England joined the rivalry too. In 1586, the English captain Sir Francis Drake sailed into St. Augustine and burned much of the town. Spain rebuilt — but the warning was clear: any country with ships could try to take Florida.

For nearly 200 more years, Spain held on to Florida while England built thirteen colonies to the north. In 1763, after a long war, England finally took Florida from Spain. The story of European Florida is not one flag's story. It is three.

The Calusa, Timucua, Apalachee, and Tequesta did not invite any of these powers. But they were the ones who lived through every battle, every fort, every flag change. Honest historians remember that.

🗺️ Florida Files Note

This lesson's file was almost labeled "Spain's Florida Story" — but that's incomplete. Florida's early colonial story involves Spain, France, and England. Using evidence, careful language, and the full picture keeps the record straight — and keeps the First Peoples in the story where they belong.

💡 Key Concept

Florida wasn't claimed by one European power — it was contested by three. Spain founded St. Augustine in 1565, France built Fort Caroline, and England eventually took the colony in 1763. Through every flag change, the First Peoples of Florida were the only ones with deep roots in the land.
