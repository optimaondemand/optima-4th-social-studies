## PROMPT 5 — ADD SPEAKER NOTES

> Add speaker notes to every slide. Speaker notes are for the TEACHER — not visible to students. Use this format for each slide:

*(You'll need to add these manually in Canva's notes panel. Here's what to write for each:)*

| Slide | Speaker Note |
|-------|-------------|
| 1. Title | "Don't rush this. Let students read. Ask: 'What do you think a Curator does?' Accept 2–3 answers before moving on." |
| 2. Museum Memo | "Read the memo dramatically — out loud, as if it just arrived. Pause after 'Your first badge awaits.' The narrative is the hook; don't rush past it." |
| 3. Hook Question | "This is a genuine philosophical question. There's no single right answer. Take 3–4 responses before adding anything yourself. Students who say 'books' or 'stories' are giving you a bridge to slide 5 (Two Wings of the Archive)." |
| 4. Four Fields | "Preview only — don't teach all four fields deeply yet. Say: 'These four fields are like the four wings of this museum. This year we'll visit all of them.' Raise Hand: let students pick a favorite and give a reason." |
| 5. Two Wings | "Primary = someone who was THERE. Secondary = someone who studied it LATER. Use an example students know: a photo of their birthday (primary) vs. a story their parent told about it years later (secondary). Chat: push for examples we haven't listed." |
| 6. First Gallery | "Walk through all four. Don't rush. The raise-hand question about 'which one could mislead us' is intentionally surprising — students often say artifacts (they can be faked) or photographs (they can be staged). Both are excellent. This answer pays off in slide 12." |
| 7. Historian's Toolkit | "Key phrase: 'Historians cannot interview someone who lived 300 years ago. They look at what that person LEFT BEHIND.' Say this slowly. It reframes history as detective work." |
| 8. Curator's Clipboard | "Show the photo. Give 60 seconds of silent observation time before taking any chat responses. Read 4–5 responses aloud. Celebrate specificity: 'I see eight people' is better than 'I see a family.'" |
| 9. Work Through the Evidence | "Take 3 minutes minimum on question 3 (Groups). Good answers: 'It shows us how people actually dressed, not just what textbooks say they wore.' Push for the word 'because' in every answer." |
| 10. Exhibit 1 & 4 | "The chat question is harder than it looks. A coin tells you: the metal existed, someone made it, it was valued. It can't tell you: what language they spoke, what they ate, how they treated each other. Push students to be specific about what's missing." |
| 11. Exhibit 2 & 3 | "The raise-hand question about the 1492 map vs. the letter has no single right answer. Both are primary sources. The lesson: primary doesn't mean unbiased. This seeds the Curator's Challenge." |
| 12. Curator's Challenge | "This is the hardest question in the lesson. Give groups 4 full minutes. Good groups will say: 'Yes — the person who made it had a reason, and they might have left things out.' That's historical thinking." |
| 13. Independent Task | "Read each step once. Ask: 'What questions do you have before I let you go?' Don't rush. Students who are unclear here disengage during async work." |
| 14. Exit Ticket | "The word 'because' is non-negotiable. Redirect anyone who stops after the first blank. Read 3–4 aloud. Strong example: 'I learned that primary sources can be misleading, because the 1492 map only showed what the explorer wanted people to see.'" |
| 15. Badge | "Let the badge sit on screen. Say: 'This badge isn't automatic. You earn it by doing the work in the lesson page. The historians who built this museum did the same thing — they showed their evidence.' Then dismiss." |

## WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE AFTER THESE FIXES

After the reorder and additions, the live class arc will be:

**Open** (slides 1–3): Title → Museum Memo → Hook Question. Students arrive, feel the museum, ask a big question.

**Build** (slides 4–7): Four Fields → Two Wings → First Gallery → Historian's Toolkit. Students get the vocabulary and framework they'll need for the analysis slides.

**Apply** (slides 8–12): Photo Analysis → Work Through Evidence → Exhibit 1&4 → Exhibit 2&3 → Curator's Challenge. Students USE the vocabulary on real sources, push into harder thinking.

**Close** (slides 13–15): Independent Task → Exit Ticket → Badge. Students know what to do next, write their evidence sentence, and see what they're working toward.

Every arc phase now has at least one interaction. No student goes more than 10 minutes without being asked to think out loud.
