Word Roots
Latin Root: urb — "city"

The Latin root urb means "city." When people gather to live, work, and govern together, they build an urb. You'll find this root in many words about how people live.

📖

urban — having to do with a city · suburb — a community near a city · suburban — relating to the suburbs · metropolis — a large city (Greek metro- + polis, "mother city")

🔎 Discovery Fact
The word "suburb" comes from sub- (under, near) + urb (city) — it literally means a community near a city. Florida has huge suburbs around Miami, Orlando, and Tampa.

Learn the Story
📖
What People Built on Florida's Map
Cities, capital, highways, farms, and borders

Look at a Florida map today and you'll see the land from yesterday — peninsula, panhandle, Everglades — but with new marks on top. Small dots show cities where many people live. A small star marks Tallahassee, the state capital, where Florida's government meets. Long lines show highways connecting one city to another. Green patches mark farms where citrus, cattle, and vegetables grow. All of these are cultural features — things that exist because people made choices about how to use the land.

Cultural features and physical features are connected. Big cities like Miami, Tampa, and Jacksonville grew up next to water — harbors made trade easier. Citrus farms cluster in central Florida where the soil is sandy and the winters are warm enough to protect the trees. Highways follow the long shape of the peninsula because that's where most people travel. When you see a city, road, or farm on a Florida map, you're reading a record of choices people made because of what the land offered.

🤔

Pause & Think: Yesterday you saw water on three sides of Florida. Today you see cities clustered on the coasts. What's the connection? Why might people choose to build big cities near the water?

Connect the Ideas
🔑 Key Concept

Cultural features — cities, capitals, highways, farms, and borders — show where people chose to live and what they decided to build. Those choices follow the physical features of the land, which is why the two maps belong together.

🦉
🌴 Opti says…
Quick observation from the archive: every coast city you see today started as a port — a place where boats could safely land. The land made the decision possible; the people made the decision. When you read any cultural map, ask both questions: what did the land allow, and what did the people choose?

2. Which is a cultural feature on a Florida map?
ALake Okeechobee
BThe Everglades wetland
CInterstate 95 highway
Correct! A highway is built by people — that makes it a cultural feature. Lakes and wetlands are made by nature, so they're physical features.
Remember: cultural features are things people made. Lakes and wetlands form naturally. Which option was built by people?
3. Why are most of Florida's biggest cities near the coast?
ABecause the land in the middle is too cold
BBecause harbors and beaches made trade and settlement easier
CBecause there are no roads in central Florida
Correct! Florida's coast offered harbors for boats and a way to trade, which is why big cities like Miami, Tampa, and Jacksonville grew up next to water.
Think about yesterday's physical map. Florida is surrounded by water on three sides. What does water near a city make easier — fishing? trading? travel?
