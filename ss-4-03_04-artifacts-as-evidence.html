Learn the Story
📖
Reading an Artifact
Observation, inference, and the famous Key Marco Cat

An artifact is any object made or used by people in the past. A pot, a spear point, a fishhook, a button, a basket, a wooden carving — all artifacts. For Florida’s first peoples, who didn’t leave written records, artifacts are how we hear them speak. The work of the historian is to listen carefully — and to be honest about what we hear.

Reading an artifact takes two different moves. The first is observation — saying exactly what you see. "The figurine is small. It is carved from wood. It has rounded ears on top of its head." Observation is just describing. The second move is inference — making a careful guess about what your observation means. "The carver probably wanted to show an animal — maybe a panther — because of the ears." Inference is allowed, but it has to be marked as a guess, not as a fact.

In 1896, an archaeologist named Frank Hamilton Cushing was digging in the wet muck of Key Marco, an island on Florida’s southwest coast — right in Calusa country. The muck preserved things wood and shell artifacts almost never survive: carved animal heads, painted masks, fishing nets, and one small wooden figurine of a kneeling cat-like figure. That figurine, now called the Key Marco Cat, is about 500 to 1,500 years old and is one of the most important pre-contact wooden artifacts ever recovered in North America.

What does the Key Marco Cat show? We can see that the Calusa carved wood beautifully, with smooth surfaces and clear shapes. We can see that they had time and skill for art, not just survival. What we can only infer: what the figure meant to the people who made it, whether it was used in a ceremony, who carved it, or whose hands held it. Those answers may never come.

That’s the honest part. One artifact can tell us a lot. But it can’t tell us everything — and a careful historian-citizen never pretends that it can.

🤔

Connect the Ideas
💡 Key Concept

Some people assume that if a group didn’t leave written records, we can’t really know much about them. Artifacts prove otherwise. Name one specific thing the Key Marco Cat tells us about the Calusa that no written record needed to.

Artifacts are objects from the past, and they are real evidence. A Florida historian reads them honestly by telling the difference between what they directly show (observation) and what we guess from them (inference).

Quick Self-Check
🧠

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. What is an artifact?
AA natural object, like a rock or a leaf, that no one made.
BAn object that was made or used by people in the past.
CA written letter or document.
Right — artifacts are objects from past people. Tools, pots, carvings, baskets, fishhooks all count.
An artifact has to involve people. Rocks and leaves are natural; written letters are documents, not objects in the same way.
2. Which statement is an observation about the Key Marco Cat, and which is an inference?
Pick the choice that has them in the right order.
AObservation: "It is a small wooden figure with rounded ears."  Inference: "Maybe it was used in a ceremony."
BObservation: "Maybe it was used in a ceremony."  Inference: "It is a small wooden figure with rounded ears."
CBoth are observations.
Exactly. "I see X" is observation. "I think X means Y" is inference. Two different moves.
Read each sentence again. Which one just describes what’s there? Which one guesses about meaning?
3. Someone claims: "If the Calusa didn’t write anything down, we can’t know much about them." Which response is most honest?
AAgree — history needs writing or it doesn’t count.
BIgnore the question.
CDisagree — artifacts like the Key Marco Cat are evidence too, and they show real skills the Calusa had.
Yes — the absence of writing isn’t the absence of evidence. Objects speak too.
Think about what we just learned from one wooden figurine. Did we need writing for any of it?
