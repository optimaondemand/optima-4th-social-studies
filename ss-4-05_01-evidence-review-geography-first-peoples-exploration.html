1
Learn the Story
📖
The Four Cards on the Wall
Read each card, then look for the connections.

The wall holds four pieces of Florida's Episode 1 story. They look like separate cards, but they are really one chain. To synthesize means to walk the chain — to see how each piece sets up the next.

1. The land came first. Florida is a long, low peninsula with two coasts, freshwater springs, wetlands, and a chain of small islands at its tip. Warm, wet, and full of food from the water — every later story depends on these facts about the land.

2. The First Peoples shaped life here. The Calusa, Tequesta, Apalachee, Timucua, and Ais lived here for thousands of years before any European arrived. They built shell mounds, traded along rivers, and matched their communities to the land's gifts and limits.

3. Europeans arrived with their own reasons. Spanish, French, and English ships came searching for gold, new routes, land to claim, and people to convert. Their reasons came from their world — but they landed in someone else's.

4. Contact changed everything — for the people already here. New tools and animals arrived. So did diseases the First Peoples had no defense against. Some communities resisted, some adapted, and many were forced from their land or lost. This is the link the wall asks us not to skip.

Connecting Episode 1: Land → People → Contact

The wall holds four pieces of evidence from the past four units. They were locked in one at a time, in different lessons. Now your job is to walk the wall and find the connections between them.

📍 Geography Evidence
Florida's land and water
A long peninsula with warm climate, freshwater springs, rich wetlands, two coastlines, and the Keys at its tip.
👣 First Peoples Evidence
Florida's earliest communities
The Calusa, Tequesta, Apalachee, Timucua, and Ais shaped the land, traded along rivers, built shell mounds, and lived here for thousands of years before any European arrived.
⛵ Exploration Evidence
European expeditions
Ponce de León, Narváez, de Soto, and others sailed for Spain, France, and England — searching for gold, for new routes, for land to claim, and for souls to convert.
⚖️ Contact Evidence
What changed (and who paid)
New goods, animals, and weapons arrived. So did diseases the First Peoples had no defense against. Some communities resisted, some adapted, and many were forced to leave or were lost.

3
Connect the Ideas
⚠️

Watch for oversimplification: Some accounts flatten four units of evidence into one tidy story — a version that quietly skips the First Peoples or treats exploration as a clean adventure with no costs. Which card on the wall makes the oversimplified version impossible? A real synthesis holds the whole picture — land, people, motives, and consequences.

💡 Key Concept

A synthesis connects evidence across many sources to tell a fuller story. In Florida's Episode 1, the connection runs in order: land shaped people → people shaped the land → exploration changed both. Honest history keeps every link in that chain visible.

Quick Self-Check

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. Which kind of evidence best helps you explain WHY a Florida tribe lived where it did?
AA list of Spanish ship names
BA map of Florida's coastlines, rivers, and food sources
CThe date a European arrived
Right. Geography evidence — coasts, rivers, food sources — explains why people built communities in specific places. The land came first; the people responded to it.
Think about which card on the wall would let you answer the question "why HERE?" — names and dates don't explain location.
2. An honest historian who finds a source that disagrees with their idea should…
AHide the source so the story stays clean
BChange the idea so it matches what the evidence shows
CIgnore the source and finish the project
Exactly. Honesty means the evidence wins, not the story we hoped to tell. That's the whole virtue of today's lesson.
Re-read the Virtue Spotlight at the top. Honest history follows the evidence, even when it complicates the idea.
3. Why do historians study geography BEFORE studying exploration?
ABecause the land shaped what explorers found and who they met when they got here
BBecause geography is easier than history
CBecause old maps are pretty
Yes. Land comes first. The reason Florida's coast attracted Spanish ships is the same reason the Calusa thrived there centuries earlier — geography sets the stage for everything else.
Look back at the Geography and First Peoples cards. What do they have in common that the Exploration card depends on?
