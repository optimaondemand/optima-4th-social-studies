Making Wise Money Choices
Income, saving, spending, credit, risk, and responsibility

Every person — and every family — makes financial choices. These choices involve earning money, deciding what to do with it, and understanding the consequences. Financial literacy means understanding how money works so you can make smart decisions. Let's look at the key concepts.

Income is money you earn. Adults earn income from their jobs — this can be a salary (a set amount per year) or wages (paid by the hour). Kids can earn income too — from chores, allowance, or small jobs. Income is the starting point for every financial decision: you can't spend, save, or give what you haven't earned.

Saving and spending. Once you have income, you make choices. Saving means setting money aside for the future — maybe for something expensive you want later, or for an emergency. Spending means using your money now to buy goods (things you can touch, like food or clothes) or services (things people do for you, like a haircut or a car repair). The trade-off is clear: money you spend now is money you can't save for later.

Credit and risk. Credit means borrowing money with a promise to pay it back — usually with interest, which is an extra fee the lender charges. A credit card lets you buy things now and pay later, but if you don't pay on time, the interest adds up and you end up paying more than the original price. Risk is the chance that a financial decision won't work out. Investing money in a business might lead to profit — or it might lead to loss. Borrowing too much on credit is risky because the debt can grow.

Responsibility. Responsible citizens make careful financial choices. They budget — planning how to divide their income between saving, spending, and giving. They avoid borrowing more than they can repay. They think about consequences before making big purchases. Financial responsibility isn't about being rich — it's about being thoughtful with whatever you have.

Unlock Challenge
Trade and Choice
Financial Choices and Responsibility

Complete a Trade and Choice task about a financial decision. Pick a scenario: a family earns $500 this week. Identify one choice they could make (save it, spend it, or use credit for a bigger purchase). Then explain: What is the benefit? What is the cost or trade-off? What is the consequence of that choice?

Start by describing the choice. Then explain the benefit — what does the family gain? Next, identify the trade-off — what do they give up? Finally, describe what might happen as a result of that choice (the consequence).
