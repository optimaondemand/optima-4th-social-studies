🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courage
Using precise historical language takes courage. The first time you say archaeology out loud, it feels long. The first time you write chronology in a notebook, it looks scary. But the right word is shorter than ten guesses. Courage is saying the word, asking what it means if you're not sure, and using it again next time. Precise words are a historian's best tool.

Florida History Vocabulary
Five words historians use most — and the roots beneath them

Over the past several days we've been collecting Florida evidence, sorting it, and building timelines. The whole time, we've also been picking up words. Today we slow down and name them. Here are five vocabulary words a Florida historian uses almost every day:

  • primary source — evidence made by someone who was there at the time. Example: a 1565 Pedro Menéndez letter.
  • secondary source — an account made later by someone studying primary sources. Example: a 2010 Florida history textbook chapter.
  • chronology — a list of events in the order they happened. Example: 1513 → 1565 → 1821 → 1845 → 1865.
  • archive — a collection of original records. Example: the State Archives of Florida.
  • archaeology — the study of ancient things people left behind. Example: digging at a Calusa shell mound in southwest Florida.

Now look at the last two. Both start with arch- — the Greek root for "old, first, original." That's not an accident. Once you know one root, the others fall open:

  • archive = "old + records" → a collection of original records
  • archaeology = "old + study" → the study of ancient things
  • archaic = "very old" → from the earliest times
  • architect = "first + builder" → the original designer of a building

The historian's title comes straight from arch-: an Archivist is "one who guards the original records." Greek hiding in plain sight.
