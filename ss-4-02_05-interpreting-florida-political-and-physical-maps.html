🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courtesy
Reading two maps at once takes courtesy — slowing down, giving each map its turn, listening to what each layer says before jumping to a conclusion. Geographers don't shout over their maps; they take notes. Today, when you study the dual map, give the physical layer its full attention, then the political layer, and only then put the two together. That's a respectful reader.

Word Roots
Greek Root: graph — "to write, to draw"

The Greek root graph means "to write" or "to draw." Every map is a kind of writing — geographers draw what they see on the earth so others can read it later.

🔎 Discovery Fact
The word geography literally means "earth-writing" — geo (earth) + graph (write). Geographers are people who write down what they see when they look at the earth.

Learn the Story
📖
Two Maps, One Florida
How physical and political maps fit together

A physical map shows what nature put there — coastlines, rivers, wetlands, hills, lakes. A political map shows what people drew — state borders, county lines, cities, capitals, highways. Each map tells a true story, but neither map tells the whole story by itself.

When geographers put the two maps together, new questions become possible. Why is the city built right there? The political map shows the city; the physical map shows the natural harbor next to it. Why does this county have so few people? The political map shows the boundary; the physical map shows the swamp inside it. Reading both layers at once is how a geographer asks questions a single map can't answer.

🤔

Pause & Think: Imagine looking at a map of your own city. What would the physical layer show? What would the political layer show? Which of your own daily decisions are shaped by each layer?

Connect the Ideas
💡 Key Concept

Florida is one place, but it takes two maps to read it well. Physical features show what nature gave; political features show what people drew. Geographers read both layers at the same time so they can ask better questions about the land and the people on it.

1. Which of these belongs on a PHYSICAL map?
AThe state capital
BA coastline
CA county border
Correct! A coastline is a natural feature — it shows where the land meets the water. Physical maps show what nature put there.
A physical map shows what NATURE made — not what PEOPLE drew. Which option is a natural feature that exists without any human decision?
2. Which of these belongs on a POLITICAL map?
AThe Everglades wetland
BLake Okeechobee
CThe Florida–Georgia border
Correct! A border is a line PEOPLE drew. Political maps show what humans decided — boundaries, capitals, and where to put cities.
A political map shows what PEOPLE drew or decided. Which option is a line that exists only because humans agreed on it?
3. Why do geographers read both maps together?
ATo choose which map is more accurate
BBecause each map only tells part of the story
CBecause political maps replaced physical maps
Correct! Each map only tells part of the story. A city on a political map makes more sense once you see the harbor or river next to it on the physical map.
Think about your own neighborhood. Would knowing ONLY the natural features help you find a friend's house? Would knowing ONLY the streets help you understand why your town is where it is?
