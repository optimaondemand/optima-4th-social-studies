Knowledge Check
⏲ ~10 min
1Segregation in Florida meant that:
  • A) All people shared the same schools and restaurants
  • B) Black and white people were forced to use separate facilities by law
  • C) Only children had to follow different rules
  • D) People chose to live apart by choice
Correct Answer: B
Segregation was a system of laws (often called Jim Crow laws) that forced Black and white people to use separate schools, restaurants, buses, drinking fountains, and other public facilities. This was not a choice — it was enforced by law and backed by the threat of punishment.
Common Misconceptions
Students who pick D may confuse segregation with voluntary separation. Emphasize that segregation was required by law, not chosen. Students who pick C may not understand that segregation affected all ages.
2The Tallahassee Bus Boycott was inspired by similar protests in:
  • A) New York City
  • B) Montgomery, Alabama
  • C) Washington, D.C.
  • D) Jacksonville, Florida
Correct Answer: B
The Tallahassee Bus Boycott of 1956 was directly inspired by the Montgomery Bus Boycott in Alabama, which began in 1955 after Rosa Parks was arrested for refusing to give up her seat. The success in Montgomery encouraged civil rights activists in Tallahassee to organize their own boycott.
Common Misconceptions
Students may pick D (Jacksonville) because it is also in Florida. Some may pick C (Washington, D.C.) because they associate it with the federal government and civil rights legislation. Remind students that the bus boycott movement specifically began in Montgomery.
3Harry T. and Harriette V. Moore are remembered for:
  • A) Building Florida’s first railroad
  • B) Fighting for voting rights and equal treatment for Black Floridians
  • C) Discovering Cape Canaveral
  • D) Starting Florida’s tourism industry
Correct Answer: B
Harry T. and Harriette V. Moore were civil rights activists in Florida who worked tirelessly to register Black voters and fight for equal treatment. They were tragically killed by a bomb placed under their home on Christmas night, 1951, making them among the first martyrs of the modern civil rights movement.
Common Misconceptions
Students may not recall the Moores if they blend them with other leaders. If students pick A or D, they are confusing the Moores with figures from Florida’s economic development. Reinforce that the Moores’ legacy is about civil rights and voting access.
4What does “desegregation” mean?
  • A) Creating new laws to keep people apart
  • B) Ending the practice of separating people by race
  • C) Moving to a new state
  • D) Starting a new school
Correct Answer: B
Desegregation means ending the enforced separation of people by race. In Florida, this included integrating schools, buses, lunch counters, and other public facilities so that Black and white people could use them together as equals.
Common Misconceptions
Students who pick A are confusing desegregation with segregation — they are opposites. The prefix “de-” means to undo or reverse, so de-segregation means undoing segregation. This is a good vocabulary teaching moment.
5A. Philip Randolph helped workers by:
  • A) Closing all factories in Florida
  • B) Organizing labor unions so workers could fight for fair pay and better conditions
  • C) Building new railroads across the state
  • D) Running for governor of Florida
Correct Answer: B
A. Philip Randolph, born in Crescent City, Florida, became one of the most important labor and civil rights leaders in American history. He organized the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters, the first major Black labor union, and later helped organize the 1963 March on Washington.
Common Misconceptions
Students may confuse Randolph with railroad builders (C) because his union was connected to the railroad industry. Clarify that he organized workers, not railroads. If students pick D, they may be confusing him with a political figure.
6Which is an example of a citizen solving a community problem through government?
  • A) Ignoring a broken sidewalk and walking around it
  • B) Attending a city council meeting to ask for a new crosswalk near a school
  • C) Moving to a different neighborhood
  • D) Complaining to friends but doing nothing
Correct Answer: B
Attending a city council meeting is an example of civic participation — a citizen using the tools of government to solve a community problem. This connects to the Unit 11 theme that citizens can work with government to make their communities safer and better.
Common Misconceptions
Students may not recognize that attending a meeting is “using government.” Help them see that government includes local meetings, not just voting or big national events. Options A, C, and D all show citizens not engaging with government to solve the problem.
7Josiah Walls was significant in Florida history because he:
  • A) Was one of Florida’s first Black members of the U.S. Congress during Reconstruction
  • B) Founded the city of Tampa
  • C) Built the first bridge across the St. Johns River
  • D) Was Florida’s last territorial governor
Correct Answer: A
Josiah Walls was born into slavery and later became one of Florida’s first Black members of the U.S. House of Representatives during Reconstruction. He served multiple terms and advocated for education, infrastructure, and civil rights for all Floridians.
Common Misconceptions
Students may confuse Walls with other historical figures or not recall his specific role. If students pick B, C, or D, they are guessing. Reinforce that Walls’s significance is as a Black elected official during a time when this was rare and groundbreaking.
8Volunteerism means:
  • A) Getting paid extra for working overtime
  • B) Freely giving your time to help others or your community without being paid
  • C) Voting in every election
  • D) Following all laws at all times
Correct Answer: B
Volunteerism is the practice of giving your time and energy to help others or improve your community without expecting payment. Examples include helping at a food bank, cleaning up a park, or tutoring younger students.
Common Misconceptions
Students may confuse volunteerism with other forms of civic duty. Voting (C) and following laws (D) are important civic responsibilities, but they are not volunteerism. The key distinction is that volunteerism is freely chosen and unpaid.

Historical Source: Florida Lunch Counter Sit-Ins (1960)

Read the following account describing the 1960 lunch counter sit-ins in Florida.

“In March 1960, a group of college students in Tallahassee walked into a Woolworth’s store and sat down at the whites-only lunch counter. They were refused service, but they stayed seated — calm, quiet, and peaceful. Some people shouted at them. Others poured food on them. But the students did not fight back. They returned the next day, and the day after that. Their peaceful protest helped show the nation that segregation was unfair. Within a few years, laws began to change.”
9Use the source above to answer the following question.
What strategy did the students use to protest segregation? Use details from the source to explain.
Model Response (Secure — 3)
The students used a peaceful protest strategy called a sit-in. They sat down at a whites-only lunch counter and refused to leave, even though they were not served. The source says they stayed “calm, quiet, and peaceful” even when people shouted at them and poured food on them. They did not fight back. They also came back the next day and the day after that, showing that they were determined to keep protesting until things changed. Their peaceful approach helped show the nation that segregation was unfair.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Identifies the strategy as peaceful protest/sit-in, cites specific details from the source (calm, quiet, peaceful; returned the next day; did not fight back), and explains why it was effective. Developing (2): Names the strategy but uses only one or two details, or does not explain effectiveness. Beginning (1): Vague answer without source details, or misidentifies the strategy.
10Think about what you read in the source.
Why do you think the students chose to remain peaceful even when people treated them badly?
Model Response (Secure — 3)
I think the students chose to remain peaceful because they knew that fighting back would hurt their cause. If they had been violent, people might have blamed them instead of seeing how unfair segregation was. By staying calm and not fighting back, they showed that they were the ones acting with dignity and respect, while the people who shouted at them and poured food on them were the ones behaving badly. Their peaceful approach helped the rest of the nation see that segregation was wrong, and within a few years, laws began to change. Being peaceful made their message stronger.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Explains a clear reason for choosing peace (moral high ground, effectiveness, showed dignity), connects it to the outcome (laws changed, nation noticed). Developing (2): Gives a reason but does not connect to outcomes or is vague. Beginning (1): One sentence with no reasoning, or says “because they were nice” without explanation.
11Connect the source to what you learned in Unit 11.
Based on this source and what you learned in Unit 11, how did ordinary citizens help bring about change in Florida?
Model Response (Secure — 3)
Ordinary citizens helped bring about change in Florida by taking action even when it was dangerous or difficult. The college students in the source protested segregation by sitting peacefully at a whites-only lunch counter, and their actions helped show the nation that these laws were unjust. In Unit 11, we also learned about the Tallahassee Bus Boycott, where everyday people refused to ride the buses until they were desegregated. Citizens like Harry T. and Harriette V. Moore fought for voting rights even though it put their lives at risk. These examples show that change does not always come from presidents or famous leaders — ordinary people who stand up for what is right can change laws and communities.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Uses source details AND connects to other Unit 11 content (boycott, Moores, Bethune, Randolph, etc.), explains how citizens drove change. Developing (2): References either the source or Unit 11 content but not both, or connection is weak. Beginning (1): No evidence from source or lessons, or only restates the question. Accept any valid Unit 11 example beyond the source.

These questions ask students to think carefully and write multi-sentence responses. Look for specific details from Unit 11 and clear reasoning.

12Read the prompt below.
Choose ONE leader from Unit 11 (Mary McLeod Bethune, the Moores, A. Philip Randolph, or Josiah Walls). Explain what challenge they faced, what they did about it, and why their actions mattered for Florida.
Model Response (Secure — 3)
Mary McLeod Bethune faced the challenge that Black children, especially girls, had very few opportunities to get an education in Florida. Schools for Black students were underfunded, and many families could not afford to send their children to school at all. Bethune did something about it by starting a school for Black girls in Daytona Beach in 1904, which eventually grew into Bethune-Cookman University. She also became an advisor to President Franklin D. Roosevelt, giving her a national platform to fight for civil rights and education. Her actions mattered for Florida because she proved that education could lift people out of poverty and that Black leaders could influence national policy. She inspired generations of Floridians to value learning and fight for equal access to education.
Scoring Notes
Accept any of the four leaders. Secure (3): Names a leader, clearly describes the challenge, explains specific actions taken, and connects to why it mattered for Florida. Developing (2): Names a leader and describes actions but the challenge or significance is vague. Beginning (1): Names a leader but provides little or no detail about challenge, actions, or significance. Other valid responses: The Moores (voting rights, NAACP organizing, tragic assassination), Randolph (labor rights, Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters, March on Washington), Walls (Black representation in Congress during Reconstruction, advocated for education).
13Read the prompt below.
How can citizens today use voting, public service, and volunteerism to make their communities better? Give at least two specific examples.
Model Response (Secure — 3)
Citizens today can use voting, public service, and volunteerism in many ways to make their communities better. For example, citizens can vote for leaders who will build new parks, fix roads, or improve schools in their neighborhood. By voting, people have a say in who makes decisions that affect their daily lives. Another example is volunteerism — a citizen could volunteer at a local food bank to help families who do not have enough to eat, or they could organize a neighborhood cleanup to make their community safer and more beautiful. Public service is also important: someone might run for the school board to help make decisions about education, or serve on a local planning committee. These actions show that citizens do not have to wait for someone else to fix problems — they can take responsibility and make a difference themselves.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Gives at least two specific, concrete examples and connects them to voting, public service, or volunteerism. Explains how these actions improve the community. Developing (2): Gives two examples but they are vague (“vote for good people”) or does not clearly connect to the three concepts. Beginning (1): Gives only one vague example or restates the question without specifics. Accept any reasonable examples that demonstrate understanding of civic participation.

Section D
Virtue Lens Reflection
⏲ ~5 min
⚖️ Virtue Focus: Courage
Courage means doing the right thing even when you are afraid. Many civil rights leaders in Florida faced real danger for standing up for what was right.
14Reflect on what you learned in Unit 11.
Many civil rights leaders in Florida faced real danger for standing up for what was right. Choose one person or group from Unit 11 and explain how they showed courage. Why is courage an important virtue for citizens?
Model Response (Secure — 3)
Harry T. and Harriette V. Moore showed incredible courage by fighting for voting rights for Black Floridians even though they knew it was dangerous. They traveled across Florida registering Black voters and speaking out against racial violence at a time when doing so could get you killed. Tragically, they were both killed when a bomb was placed under their home on Christmas night, 1951. Despite the threats and danger, they never stopped working for justice. Courage is an important virtue for citizens because standing up for what is right is not always easy or safe. Without courage, people might stay silent when they see something unfair, and nothing would ever change. The Moores’ courage inspired others to keep fighting for civil rights even after they were gone.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3): Names a specific person or group, describes how they showed courage with specific details, and explains why courage matters for citizens generally. Developing (2): Names an example and connects to courage but explanation of why courage matters is vague or missing. Beginning (1): No specific example, or no connection to courage. Accept any valid example: The lunch counter students (faced hostility peacefully), Bethune (started a school against great odds), Randolph (organized workers despite opposition), Walls (served in Congress when Black representation was dangerous), the Tallahassee Bus Boycott participants.
