## SYSTEM PROMPT (paste this into Canva AI)

> Design a 15-slide live class presentation for 3rd grade social studies. The visual theme is a **museum at night** — dark, dramatic, immersive. Every slide should feel like a different gallery room inside a living museum called the **Curator Training Hall**.
>
> **Visual language:**
> - Dark backgrounds throughout (#07101F deep navy-black, or dark charcoal)
> - Gold/amber accent colors for headers, borders, and interactive cues (#C8A84B, #D4943A)
> - Off-white or parchment for placard text areas (#F5EDD8)
> - Deep teal for action elements (#1A8A7D)
> - Subtle grid or texture overlays that feel like aged stone or wood paneling
> - "Display case" card layouts: dark recessed boxes with gold borders and a warm amber spotlight glow behind center objects
> - Rope barrier accents along the bottom of select slides (horizontal gold line with small post caps)
> - No gradients that look like PowerPoint defaults. Think Smithsonian after dark.
>
> **Slide structure rules:**
> - Title slides: Full dark background, large serif title (Cambria or similar), small gold unit banner at top, lesson code and subtitle at bottom
> - Content slides: Dark background with 1–3 content cards or a clear information hierarchy
> - Interactive slides: Include a clearly labeled action box — color-coded by interaction type:
>   - TEAL box = "In the Chat" (Microsoft Teams chat)
>   - GREEN box = "Raise Your Hand" (Teams raise hand)
>   - TERRACOTTA/RUST box = "In Your Groups" (Teams Breakout Rooms)
> - Action boxes must have a bold header label, a thin divider line below it, and the student prompt in the body
> - No polls. No annotation. Teams only: Chat, Raise Hand, Breakout Rooms.
> - Speaker notes on every slide (teacher language, not student-facing)
>
> **Tone:** Scholarly but warm. Students are "Junior Curators" — apprentice historians, not test-takers. The museum is real. The work matters. Every slide should make a 3rd grader feel like they've been trusted with something important.
>
> **Backward planning:** This presentation is designed backward from the exit ticket. The last real content slide is the exit ticket prompt. Everything before it is scaffolding that builds exactly the skills the exit ticket requires — no more, no less. Every slide earns its place.

## SLIDE-BY-SLIDE OUTLINE — LESSON 3.01.01
### "What Historians Study" — Curator Training Hall, Unit 1, Week 1

---

### **Why backward planning?**

The exit ticket (Slide 6) asks: *"In this exhibit, I learned that ___ because ___."*

That sentence requires:
1. A fact the student learned (content knowledge)
2. Evidence to back it up (reasoning from the lesson)

So everything before Slide 6 must build those two things:
- **Content knowledge:** what historians are, what tools they use, what primary vs. secondary sources are
- **Evidence-based reasoning:** the practice of noticing → describing → interpreting

The slides are ordered so each one hands the next one something to work with.

---

### SLIDE 1 — Title / Entrance
**Purpose:** Orient. Create atmosphere. Signal this is not a normal class.

**Content:**
- Top banner (small caps, gold): `CURATOR TRAINING HALL  ·  UNIT 1  ·  WEEK 1`
- Main title (large serif, white, 3 lines): `What Historians Study`
- Subtitle pill/badge: `Junior Curator  ·  Evidence Keeper Badge`
- Lesson code (small, amber): `Lesson 3.01.01  ·  3rd Grade Social Studies`
- Rope barrier along bottom

**Visual:** Full dark background. Amber spotlight oval centered behind the title. No other elements. The title should feel like a museum entrance sign.

**Speaker note:** "Don't rush the title slide. Let students read it. Ask: 'What do you think a Curator does?' Accept 2–3 answers before moving on."

---

### SLIDE 2 — Two Wings of the Archive
**Purpose:** Introduce the core vocabulary before students need it (primary vs. secondary sources). Plant the concept; the rest of the lesson waters it.

**Content:**
Two side-by-side display cases, each with:

**WING A — PRIMARY SOURCES**
- Created AT THE TIME by someone who was THERE
- Examples: the photograph we'll examine today, a diary entry, an artifact

**WING B — SECONDARY SOURCES**
- Created LATER by someone who STUDIED what happened
- Examples: a history textbook, a documentary, an encyclopedia article

Below both cases: "Historians use BOTH. But they treat them differently."

**Visual:** Two dark display cases with gold borders, amber spotlight glow, parchment placard at the bottom of each with the label. Equal size. Strong visual symmetry.

**Speaker note:** "Don't define these too deeply yet. Say: 'We're going to come back to these words. For now, just remember: primary = someone who was THERE, secondary = someone who studied it later.'"

---

### SLIDE 3 — The Curator's Clipboard (Enter the Scene)
**Purpose:** Primary source analysis practice — the skill the exit ticket will test. Students practice noticing before interpreting. This is the lesson's core intellectual move.

**Content:**
- Large card/clipboard design, slightly tilted — feels like a real form
- Top label: `CURATOR'S CLIPBOARD`
- Below: `Primary Source Photograph  ·  c. 1900s`
- Three prompt rows:
  1. **What do you NOTICE?** (describe only — no guessing)
  2. **What do you WONDER?** (questions this source raises)
  3. **What QUESTION would you ask?** (if you could meet the person in it)
- Action box (TEAL): `In the Chat — Type one thing you NOTICE. Describe only. No guessing yet.`

**Visual:** The clipboard is the hero element — parchment background, dark border, gold header. The rest of the slide is dark.

**Speaker note:** "This is the most important slide in the deck. Take 3 full minutes here. Read the three prompts aloud. Distinguish between notice ('I see a woman in a long dress') and interpret ('she looks rich'). Collect 5–6 chat responses. Celebrate specificity."

---

### SLIDE 4 — Work Through the Evidence
**Purpose:** Move from noticing to interpreting — guided by the teacher. This bridges slide 3 (observation) to the deeper thinking that will complete the exit ticket sentence.

**Content:**
Three questions, scaffolded in order of difficulty:

1. **What TYPE of source is this? How do you know?**
   → Raise Your Hand (GREEN action box)

2. **List THREE specific things you can see. Describe — don't interpret.**
   → In the Chat (TEAL action box)

3. **What does this source tell us that a textbook couldn't?**
   → In Your Groups (TERRACOTTA action box) — 3 minutes, then share out

**Visual:** Three stacked action boxes, color-coded. Clean, uncluttered. The three boxes ARE the slide.

**Speaker note:** "Q3 is the payoff. Good answers sound like: 'It shows us how people actually dressed, not just what textbooks say they wore.' Push for specifics. This is evidence-based reasoning in miniature."

---

### SLIDE 5 — Your Independent Curator Task
**Purpose:** Transition to async work. Set up what students will do independently in the OAO lesson page. This slide is a bridge — the live class hands off to the asynchronous lesson.

**Content:**
- Header: `Your Independent Curator Task`
- Subheader: `Open the lesson page and work through all four tabs:`
- Four numbered items with icons:
  1. 📍 Enter the Museum — Read the Museum Memo and Curator's Question. Review vocabulary.
  2. 🔍 Examine the Evidence — Work through all four display cases.
  3. 📝 Record Your Evidence — Complete your Curator's Chronicle entries.
  4. 🏅 Claim Your Badge — Submit your work to earn the Evidence Keeper Badge.
- Small note at bottom: "You have [X] days to complete the lesson page before our next live class."

**Visual:** Four steps in a numbered row or column — each with a distinct icon, clean left-aligned text. Background is still dark but slightly lighter panel behind the four steps.

**Speaker note:** "Read each step once. Ask: 'What questions do you have before I let you go?' Don't rush. Students who are unclear here will disengage during async work."

---

### SLIDE 6 — Exit Ticket (Curator's Chronicle)
**Purpose:** The destination the whole lesson was built toward. Students synthesize what they learned into a structured sentence that requires both content knowledge and a reason.

**Content:**
- Header: `Curator's Chronicle — Exit Ticket`
- Sentence frame (large, centered, parchment card):
  > "In this exhibit, I learned that ___________
  > because ___________."
- Action box (TEAL): `In the Chat — Type your completed sentence before leaving.`
- Small note: "Every Curator's Chronicle entry starts with evidence. 'Because' is required."

**Visual:** The sentence frame is the hero — parchment card, dark border, gold label at top reading `CURATOR'S CHRONICLE`. Centered on the slide. Everything else steps back.

**Speaker note:** "Read a few aloud. The 'because' is non-negotiable — redirect anyone who stops after the first blank. Strong example: 'I learned that primary sources can tell us things textbooks can't, because the photograph showed details no one would have written down.' Celebrate that kind of response."

---

### SLIDE 7 — Coming Up / Badge Preview
**Purpose:** Motivate. Show students what they're working toward. End the class on forward momentum, not a full stop.

**Content:**
- Badge visual: gold medallion, "Evidence Keeper" label
- Three bullet points beneath it:
  - Master the 4 historian's tools
  - Explain primary vs. secondary sources
  - Complete the Curator's Chronicle
- Rope barrier at bottom

**Visual:** The badge is centered, lit by an amber spotlight, sitting in a dark display case. It looks earned, not given. Aspirational — not decorative.

**Speaker note:** "Don't rush this slide. Let the badge sit on screen. Say: 'This badge isn't automatic. You earn it by doing the work in the lesson page. The historians who built this museum did the same thing — they showed their evidence.' Then dismiss."

## DESIGN NOTES FOR CANVA

- **Font pairing:** Cambria or similar classic serif for headers / Calibri or similar humanist sans for body
- **Do not use default Canva templates.** The museum aesthetic needs custom dark backgrounds — choose a blank dark canvas and build up.
- **Card/shape approach:** Use rounded rectangles with dark fill + colored stroke for display cases. Use ovals with 80%+ transparency for spotlight glows.
- **Action boxes:** Consistent format across all slides. Build one → duplicate → recolor. Label always at top, divider line, prompt below.
- **The rope:** A horizontal line (2–2.5pt, gold) with small oval caps at each end. Appears on title slide, exit ticket, badge slide — it closes the "gallery visit."
- **Slide transitions:** None, or a simple fade. The drama is in the design, not the animation.

---

## WHAT MAKES THIS BETTER THAN A STANDARD SLIDE DECK

Standard decks present content. This deck **builds toward one thing**: a student who can complete the sentence *"I learned that ___ because ___"* with genuine evidence.

Every slide either:
1. Gives students something to put in the first blank (content knowledge), or
2. Practices the thinking skill that fills the second blank (evidence-based reasoning)

If a slide doesn't do one of those two things, it shouldn't exist.

That's backward planning. Start with the exit ticket. Build everything else to earn it.
