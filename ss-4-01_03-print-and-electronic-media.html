🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Honesty
Honesty stays with us. Today we widen it. Print sources have a publisher, an author, and a date printed right on them. Electronic sources also have those things — but sometimes you have to dig to find them, and sometimes they're missing. An honest Florida historian doesn't just trust a source because it looks fancy. We ask: who made it, when, and where is it from? If we can't answer those three, we say so.

Print & Electronic Sources
Same evidence, different delivery

Yesterday we learned that Florida sources come in two big groups: primary and secondary. Today we learn that a source can also reach us in two different ways: as a print source or as an electronic source.

Print sources are anything you can hold in your hands. Examples from Florida history:

  • A Florida history textbook
  • A 1920 newspaper from Jacksonville
  • A printed photograph from the State Archives of Florida
  • A historical map of St. Augustine

Electronic sources are anything that lives on a screen. Examples:

  • The Florida Memory website (floridamemory.com), which posts thousands of scanned photos and documents
  • A National Park Service video about the Castillo de San Marcos
  • A podcast about the Seminole
  • A digital map you can zoom in on

Here's the tricky part: the same source can show up in both forms. A Pedro Menéndez letter from 1565 is a primary source whether you read it in a printed book OR see a scanned image on a website. The kind of source doesn't change. The delivery does.

So how do we trust a source? The historian uses three questions, every time:

🔍

The Three-Question Check: Who made it? · When? · Where is it from? If you can't answer all three, say so — and keep looking.

💡 Key Concept

Florida history reaches us as print sources (books, newspapers, photos) and electronic sources (websites, videos, databases). Both can be useful — but only if we run the three-question check: who made it, when, and where is it from?

2. You find a Florida history fact on a website. The page doesn't say who wrote it or when. What should a Florida historian do?
ATrust it anyway — it's on the internet, so it must be checked already
BThrow it out — anything online is automatically wrong
CSay honestly that we don't know who made it or when, and keep looking for a source we can check
Exactly. We run the three-question check on every source. If we can't answer who, when, and where, we say so — and we keep looking.
Think back to today's virtue spotlight. Honest historians don't just trust or dismiss a source — they ask questions.
3. A Pedro Menéndez letter from 1565 can be read in a printed book OR seen as a scan on the Florida Memory website. What does that show about sources?
AThe print version is real history and the website version is fake
BThe same source can reach us in different ways — what changes is the delivery, not the source itself
CThe letter must have been written twice
Yes. The kind of source (primary or secondary) stays the same. The delivery (print or electronic) can change. Both versions of the letter are useful.
Re-read the Key Concept. The kind of source doesn't change just because the delivery changes — only the way we receive it does.
