⚓ Virtue Spotlight — Courage
Courage means doing something hard or dangerous because it matters. Enslaved people who fled British colonies and traveled hundreds of miles through swamps, forests, and danger to reach Spanish Florida showed extraordinary courage. The people who built Fort Mose showed courage too — they created a community and defended it. As you study today, notice how courage appears in this story from many directions.

Teacher recording notes: Walk students through three reasons Spanish explorers sailed toward Florida: gold and riches, claiming land for Spain, and spreading Catholic faith (often called the "three G's: gold, glory, God"). Name three expeditions in order: Juan Ponce de León landed in 1513 and named the land "La Florida." Pánfilo de Narváez landed in 1528 and his expedition mostly failed. Hernando de Soto arrived in 1539 with a large force and traveled far inland. Be honest that these expeditions caused enormous harm to the people already living in Florida — they brought disease, violence, and disruption. Florida historians always keep both stories — the explorers' side and the Native peoples' side. Aim for 4–5 minutes. Calm, factual tone.

Learn the Story
📖
Fort Mose: Freedom in Spanish Florida
The first free Black settlement and what it tells us about courage

In the late 1600s and early 1700s, the British colonies in the Carolinas and Georgia relied on enslaved labor. Enslaved African and African American people were forced to work without pay, without freedom, and without legal rights. But some of them heard about a place to the south — Spanish Florida — where Spain had promised freedom to those who escaped British territory and converted to Catholicism.

Why Spain offered freedom. Spain and Britain were rivals. Spain wanted to weaken the British colonies and strengthen its own settlements. In 1693, the Spanish king issued a royal decree: enslaved people who reached Spanish Florida and accepted the Catholic faith would be declared free. This was not generosity for its own sake — it was strategy. But for the people who made the journey, the promise was real.

The journey south. Escaping was dangerous. People traveled on foot through swamps, rivers, and forests — often at night. Some traveled alone; others moved in small groups. They faced hunger, exhaustion, snakes, alligators, and the constant threat of capture. Many did not survive. Those who reached St. Augustine were taken in by Spanish officials and given land near the city.

Fort Mose. In 1738, Spanish Florida established Gracia Real de Santa Teresa de Mose — known today as Fort Mose — about two miles north of St. Augustine. It was the first legally sanctioned free Black settlement in what is now the United States. About 100 free Black men, women, and children lived there. They built homes, grew food, worshipped at a church, and trained as militia to help defend St. Augustine.

Captain Francisco Menéndez. Fort Mose was led by Francisco Menéndez, a formerly enslaved man who had escaped from the Carolinas. He became a militia captain and defender of Fort Mose. When British forces attacked in 1740, Menéndez and the Fort Mose militia fought back in the Battle of Fort Mose — one of the first armed conflicts involving free Black soldiers in North American history.

Why Fort Mose matters. Fort Mose shows that the story of freedom in America is older, more complicated, and more courageous than many people realize. Long before the American Revolution, people risked everything for freedom — and built a real community where it could exist.

Connect the Ideas
💡 Key Concept

Spanish explorers came to Florida looking for gold, land, and converts. Juan Ponce de León named it La Florida in 1513, but Florida was already home to the Calusa, Tequesta, Apalachee, and Timucua. A good historian tells both sides of this story.

🦉
🌴 Opti says…
Quick note from the archive: the word explore comes from the Latin explorare, meaning "to search out by crying or calling." Spanish sailors sometimes shouted to test how close the coast was, listening for echoes. Florida historians "explore" by calling out questions to the evidence — and listening carefully for what answers back.
