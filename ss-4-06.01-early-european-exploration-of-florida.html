Teacher recording notes: Walk students through three reasons Spanish explorers sailed toward Florida: gold and riches, claiming land for Spain, and spreading Catholic faith (often called the "three G's: gold, glory, God"). Learn the Story
📖
Why Europeans Sailed for Florida
Three reasons, three expeditions, two sides of the story

In the early 1500s, Spain was the most powerful kingdom in Europe. Spanish ships sailed across the Atlantic looking for new lands, new wealth, and new converts to their religion. Three reasons pulled them toward Florida.

1. Gold and riches. Spanish leaders had heard stories of cities full of gold somewhere in North America. Many explorers risked their lives hoping to find one of them. Most of those stories turned out to be untrue, but the hope was powerful.

2. Land to claim for Spain. European kingdoms competed to claim large pieces of the Americas. A captain who reached new land could claim it for Spain — even if people already lived there.

3. Spreading Catholic faith. Spain saw itself as a Catholic kingdom. Many expeditions brought priests who wanted to teach Native peoples about Christianity. Sometimes this was peaceful; often it was forced.

The first three expeditions. Juan Ponce de León sailed from Puerto Rico and reached Florida's east coast in 1513. He named the land La Florida — "the flowery one" — because of its bright spring plants. Pánfilo de Narváez arrived in 1528 with hundreds of men, but storms, hunger, and conflict destroyed the expedition. Hernando de Soto landed near Tampa Bay in 1539 and marched far inland with horses and armored soldiers.

The other side of the story. Florida was already home. The Calusa, Tequesta, Apalachee, and Timucua peoples lived along Florida's coasts and rivers — they had towns, leaders, trade routes, and traditions stretching back thousands of years. When Spanish ships arrived, they brought diseases like smallpox that Native peoples had never faced. Whole villages became sick. There was also violence on both sides. A careful historian remembers both stories.

🤔

Pause & Think: Of the three reasons Europeans sailed for Florida (gold, land, faith), which one do you think mattered most to the explorers themselves? What evidence makes you think so?

Source Study
A 1500s Map of "La Florida"
🖼️ A 16th-century Spanish or European map showing Florida as "La Florida" — usually drawn with an exaggerated peninsula, sea monsters or ships in the surrounding waters, and Spanish or Latin labels for early settlements. Decorative cartouches and a compass rose are common. Suggested source: Library of Congress (loc.gov) Map Division — search "Florida 1500s" or "La Florida 1591"; Florida Memory (floridamemory.com) historic maps collection; Smithsonian Open Access (si.edu/openaccess) — search "early Florida map."

Connect the Ideas
💡 Key Concept

Spanish explorers came to Florida looking for gold, land, and converts. Juan Ponce de León named it La Florida in 1513, but Florida was already home to the Calusa, Tequesta, Apalachee, and Timucua. A good historian tells both sides of this story.

🌴 Opti says…
Quick note from the archive: the word explore comes from the Latin explorare, meaning "to search out by crying or calling." Spanish sailors sometimes shouted to test how close the coast was, listening for echoes. Florida historians "explore" by calling out questions to the evidence — and listening carefully for what answers back.

1. Who reached Florida's east coast in 1513 and gave the land its name?
AHernando de Soto
BPánfilo de Narváez
CJuan Ponce de León
Right! Juan Ponce de León sailed from Puerto Rico and reached Florida's east coast in 1513. He named the land La Florida.
Look again at the year — 1513. De Soto came later (1539), and Narváez came in 1528. The first one is who named Florida.
2. Which of these was NOT a main reason Spain sent explorers to Florida?
ATo look for gold and riches
BTo take photographs for Spanish newspapers
CTo claim land and spread Catholic faith
Right. Cameras and newspapers didn't exist yet in the 1500s. The real reasons were gold, land, and faith.
Think about what existed in the 1500s. Which option mentions a technology that wasn't invented for almost 300 more years?
3. Florida was NOT empty when Spanish ships arrived. Which Native peoples already lived there?
AThe Calusa, Tequesta, Apalachee, and Timucua
BNo one — Florida was empty
CPilgrims from Massachusetts
Right. The Calusa, Tequesta, Apalachee, and Timucua had lived in Florida for thousands of years before any Spanish ship arrived.
Look again at the lesson. Florida was NOT empty. And the Pilgrims arrived in Massachusetts — far north of Florida.
